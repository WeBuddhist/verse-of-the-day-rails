## Disambiguated Meaning
Grounded in the Chinese source (^pT02p0573a0516): 雖身有病 — "though the body has sickness"; 令心無病 — "let the mind be without sickness." Spoken by the Buddha to Nakulapitā, an elderly householder in poor health: bodily illness need not become illness of the heart-mind. The lead-in makes the point that clinging to the body brings only a fleeting comfort, so the wise do not stake their peace on it. A gentle, practical teaching in the face of pain and aging: tend the mind's wellbeing even when the body suffers.

- Rendering choice: 病 → "sick / sickness" (kept parallel across body and mind); 令 → "let" (an instruction, not a guarantee). 心 → "mind / heart" (termbase: *citta/mano* = mind, heart). Keep the two clauses balanced (body sick / mind not sick).
