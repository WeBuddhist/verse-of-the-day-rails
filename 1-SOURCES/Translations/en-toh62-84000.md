# The Noble Mahāyāna Sūtra The Questions of Rāṣṭrapāla (1)

> **84000 English, stored verbatim as a reference layer** (CC BY-NC-ND — do not excerpt or adapt for output; the shipped verse is our own translation, citing 84000). Tibetan source: `1-SOURCES/Text/bo-toh62.md`. Folio anchors `^F<folio>` align to the Degé Kangyur.

Homage to all buddhas and bodhisattvas! ^F227b

Thus have I heard at one time. In Rājagṛha, on the Vulture’s Peak, the Bhagavān was residing together with a great assembly of one thousand two hundred fifty monks and five thousand bodhisattvas, whose eloquence was unimpeded, who were endowed with patience, who had conquered the hostile māras, who were very close to realizing all the buddha qualities, who were impeded by only one birth, who had attained concentration and retention, who had reached limitless eloquence and unimpeded fearlessness, who had obtained magical power and the ultimate perfection of power, [n1] and who had appropriated all inexhaustible collections of good qualities without exception. The bodhisattvas present included the bodhisattva mahāsattvas Samantabhadra, Samantanetra, Samantāvalokita, Samantaraśmi, Samantaprabha, Uttaramati, Vardhamānamati, Anantamati, Vipulamati, Akṣayamati, Dharaṇīdhara, Jagatīṃdhara, Jayamati, Viśeṣamati, and Dhāraṇīśvararāja. In addition, the sixty unequaled bodhisattvas headed by Mañjuśrī; the sixteen noble men headed by Bhadrapāla; Brahmā, lord of the Sahā world; Śakra, lord of the gods; the four guardians of the world; the god Susīma; and the god Susthitamati—along with all lords of the gods, lords of the nāgas, lords of the kinnaras, lords of the gandharvas, lords of the yakṣas, lords of the asuras, and lords of the garuḍas, all with their retinues of hundreds of thousands—had assembled and taken their seats. ^F228a

The Bhagavān was seated on the lion throne at the seat of enlightenment, [n2] towering above the whole assembled retinue like Mount Meru. Illuminating the entire world like the sun, and all beings like the moon, he remained perfectly at peace like Brahmā. His body was difficult to approach like the body of Śakra, and he was endowed with the seven precious branches of enlightenment like a cakravartin. Like a lion, he proclaimed that all phenomena have no self and are empty. He was endowed with a body that illuminates the whole world like a huge mass of flames, his radiance blazing brightly like the king of precious jewels among the entire assortment of precious jewels, which is the splendor of all gods. [n3] Pervading the trichiliocosm with his splendor, he had become swift [n4] in determining the meaning, and had perfectly attained all excellent qualities. He resided in the assembly, intoned the melodious Brahmā voice, and taught the Dharma, endowed with a speech that makes all sentient beings understand. He accurately taught pure conduct, the Dharma that is [n5] good in the beginning, good in the middle, and good in the end, which is of good meaning, in good words, unadulterated, completely perfect, completely pure, and completely purified.

Then the bodhisattva mahāsattva named Prāmodyarāja joined the assembly, was seated, and beheld the Bhagavān seated on the lion throne—the one abiding in splendor outshining the whole gathering of the retinue, with rays of light surpassing a thousand suns. With a happy, delighted, and faithfully yearning mind, Prāmodyarāja rose from his seat, joined his palms, and praised the Bhagavān with the following appropriate verses:

“Victorious One, you shine like a golden mountain;<br>With your splendor, you outshine beings—<br>The hosts of gods, asuras, kinnaras,<br>Nāgas, śrāvakas, and sons of the buddhas. {1} ^F228b

“Just as Mount Meru, the dwelling place of hosts of gods,<br>Is beautiful, though located in the middle of the ocean,<br>Out of compassion [n6] you dwell in the middle of the ocean of suffering,<br>Sending forth hundreds of thousands of light rays. {2}

“Just as Brahmā is magnificent residing in his Brahmā states,<br>Presiding over his Brahmā realm,<br>So you, excellent being, residing in meditative absorption, liberation, and concentration,<br>Illuminate the entire world. {3}

“Just as Śakra, residing among the gods in the Heaven of the Thirty-Three,<br>Is gorgeous in all his splendor,<br>So you, king of sages, who is adorned with the buddha marks and rich in the qualities of wisdom,<br>Illuminate the entire world. {4}

“Just as the luminous king of the four continents<br>Shines [n7] in the world, making it beautiful,<br>So you, possessing a mind of compassionate intent, {5}<br>Make beings enter the noble path, making them beautiful.

“Just as the sun, shining brightly in the sky,<br>Eclipses the light of the fire jewel, [n8]<br>So you, Buddha-sun, shine in this world,<br>With your light exceeding a thousand suns. {6}

“Just as the stainless moon shines at midnight,<br>Completely pure one, illuminating the entire world,<br>So your face, Victorious One, resembles the full moon,<br>Outshining all light, radiating exquisitely. {7}

“Just as a blazing fire on a mountain peak<br>Illuminates the serene night,<br>So you have conquered the total darkness of ignorance,<br>And your wisdom light, great sage, shines forth. {8}

“Just as the lion’s persistent roar, echoing in the mountain ravines,<br>Frightens the herds of deer on the earth,<br>So the lord of men proclaims emptiness and no-self,<br>And frightens the rival tīrthikas. {9}

“Just as the genuine king of jewels glows brightly,<br>And vividly outshines all other jewels,<br>So the body of the Victorious One, in its golden color,<br>Sparkles brilliantly, outshining the whole world. {10}

“A being equal or superior to you<br>Does not exist anywhere in the world.<br>There is no one to equal you in terms of merit,<br>Wisdom, diligence, skillful means, or all qualities. {11} ^F229a

“I have seen the hero of men who illuminates the world,<br>The ocean of qualities, the protector.<br>With respect and full of joy,<br>I bow to the soles of the feet of the Victorious One. {12}

“I have praised the source of all qualities, the lamp of the world,<br>The one whose reputation is well founded and who possesses a vast intellect.<br>May all beings reach supreme enlightenment<br>Through the merit that has thus been attained.” {13}

Then, having praised the Bhagavān with these verses, the bodhisattva mahāsattva Prāmodyarāja joined his palms and, without blinking, gazed at the body of the Tathāgata. This caused him to investigate the dharmadhātu itself. He penetrated the dharmadhātu, which is profound, difficult to fathom, difficult to see, difficult to internalize, impossible to analyze, not reached by reasoning, peaceful, and subtle. He investigated the inconceivable experiential sphere of the Buddha.

He was made to fully understand that the wisdom of the Tathāgata extends to all phenomena. He accurately observed that the sphere of the buddhas equals the unequaled. He penetrated the experiential sphere, which is the object of the Tathāgata’s skillful means. He realized that the illustrious buddhas are immersed in the unique nature of the dharmadhātu, and he accurately observed the illustrious buddhas whose experiential spheres are like space, without a basis.

He became convinced that all phenomena are contained in the limit of existence, whose nature is without limit, and he came to strongly desire the unobscured liberation of a buddha. He understood that the bodies of the illustrious buddhas are permanent, peaceful, and eternal, and that the bodies of the tathāgatas completely pervade all the limitless buddhafields and manifest to all sentient beings. He was made to recall that the qualities of the illustrious buddhas do not reach an end, even at the endpoints of future eons.  Investigating the dharmadhātu itself, the bodhisattva mahāsattva Prāmodyarāja stood in silence. ^F229b

Meanwhile, the venerable Rāṣṭrapāla had promised to dwell in Śrāvastī for the three months of the rainy season. When the three months had passed and his robes had been made and received, he took up his bowl and robes, and together with the assembly of fully ordained monks and the new ones—beginners who had recently become renunciants—he proceeded to roam the country, walking toward the great city of Rājagṛha and Vulture’s Peak.

The venerable Rāṣṭrapāla proceeded to the Bhagavān’s dwelling place, and having reached him, he bowed with his head to the Bhagavān’s feet. After circumambulating the Bhagavān three times, he sat down to one side. Seated to one side, the venerable Rāṣṭrapāla joined his palms and praised the Bhagavān with the following verses:

“I pay homage to you, supreme one among men, illuminator.<br>I pay homage to you whose mind is like the sky.<br>I pay homage to you, Victorious One who eradicates doubts.<br>I pay homage to you, Sage who has gone beyond the three worlds. [n9] {14}

“In great millions of buddhafields, the guides proclaim your glory.<br>Having heard this, the sons of the victorious ones [n10]<br>Arrived here, [n11] overjoyed to worship you, O Sage,<br>You, the one endowed with an ocean of qualities. {15}

“Having thus worshiped you in a way befitting a sugata,<br>They listen to the immaculate Dharma from you, great Sage,<br>Praise your garland of qualities,<br>And return to their respective buddhafields with their minds overjoyed. {16}

“While you were seeking supreme, excellent enlightenment,<br>You acted for the sake of sentient beings,<br>Over inconceivable, myriad [n12] eons.<br>Without your mind ever becoming exhausted, {17}

“O guide, you practiced generosity and discipline,<br>You trained in patience, diligence, and meditative absorption, and<br>You perfected insight, skillful means, and power—<br>Therefore, I pay homage to you, the great guide. {18} ^F230a

“You are skilled in the bases of magical power and in excellent superknowledge,<br>Your sense faculties are trained in strengths and liberation,<br>And you have internalized the conduct for all sentient beings—<br>I pay homage to you, ocean of unequaled wisdom. {19}

“Bhagavān, supreme one among men,<br>You know the mindstreams of sentient beings very well,<br>What their conduct is, and how their karma arises.<br>You know by which methods they will be liberated. {20}

“And you eliminate desire and hatred, which have ignorance as their origin, [n13]<br>And which causes sentient beings go to the three lower realms.<br>You know the karma by which they may go to the higher realms—<br>The right and the wrong karma performed by living beings. {21}

“You know all the sugatas—<br>Those who were benefactors for the world in the past,<br>Those who are worshiped by gods and humans in the present,<br>And the future ones who will have perfected supreme virtues. {22}

“You know very well<br>The pure buddhafields and the excellent retinues<br>Of bodhisattvas and śrāvakas,<br>And the lifespans of all the great sages. {23}

“You know how the Dharma will remain after you have entered nirvāṇa,<br>How the Victorious One’s relics will be praised,<br>And how the Dharma treasure will be preserved.<br>All of this is known by you, the most excellent of men. {24}

“Homage to you, Victorious One, ocean of wisdom,<br>Who has always—through the three times [n14]—realized the wisdom of one who has the ten strengths,<br>Who has no obscurations,<br>And who is not attached to any phenomenon. {25}

“There is no one equal to you, let alone anyone superior.<br>Your body is thoroughly adorned with the buddha marks,<br>Like the sky adorned with stars.<br>I pay homage to you, the supreme one among men, supreme sage. {26}

“Even your bodily form is unequaled and captivating,<br>Outshining beings, including the gods.<br>Brahmā, Śakra, and the Akaniṣṭha gods<br>Do not seem beautiful in your presence. {27}

“You are stainless like the golden mountain,<br>And the hair [n15] on your head, glossy and smooth, spirals clockwise.<br>The uṣṇīṣa on your head produced by your extensive merit<br>Shines vividly like Mount Meru, the king of mountains. {28}

“The beautiful hair tuft between your eyebrows<br>Radiates myriad light rays.<br>Your eyes, with which you regard beings with compassionate intent,<br>Are as captivating as the blue lotus. {29} ^F230b

“Your face, O guide,<br>Is as bright as the full moon in the clear sky.<br>People cannot get enough of seeing you—<br>I pay homage to the supreme one among men with the handsome face. {30}

“Your gait is like that of a goose, of a peacock,<br>And that of the king of deer.<br>Walking slowly like a confident elephant, you cause the earth to tremble.<br>I pay homage to you, one who is steadfast in the ten strengths and firm observance. {31}

“The fingers on your hands are long, round, beautiful,<br>And adorned with webbing in between, and your fingernails are the color of pure copper.<br>When you stand up, your hands reach your knees.<br>I pay homage to you, whose body is of golden color. {32}

“When walking, you adorn the ground with beautiful footprints,<br>Embellished with the marks of wheels.<br>Ripened by the light rays shining from your footprints,<br>People go to the gods’ realm upon their deaths. {33}

“King of the Dharma, bestower of the seven riches,<br>Benefactor of the Dharma, having a tamed mind,<br>You teach beings through your Dharmic conduct.<br>I pay homage to the guide, master of the Dharma. {34}

“Your armor of loving kindness, your supreme sword of mindfulness,<br>Your bow of discipline, and your arrows of insight and skillful means<br>Completely destroy the enemies—<br>Afflictions that increase the craving for birth, death, and existence. {35}

“Having gone beyond, you liberate myriad beings;<br>Being liberated, you liberate the world from its chains.<br>You also show the path that is delightful and without sickness,<br>And on which the sugatas go to a state of peace. {36}

“Out of compassion, you teach<br>This unconditioned peaceful supreme state,<br>In which there is neither birth nor death,<br>Nor the occurrence of suffering from deprivation. {37}

“May beings become completely enlightened through the merit<br>I have gathered by praising the Victorious One,<br>Who has crossed over to the other side through having mastered all phenomena,<br>Who is the great sage, the supreme one in this world.” {38}

Then, having praised the Bhagavān with these verses, the venerable Rāṣṭrapāla joined his palms.  Rising from his seat, he draped his upper garment over one shoulder and placed his right knee on the ground. He then bowed toward the Bhagavān with joined hands and made a request to the Bhagavān, “If you, Bhagavān, allowed me the opportunity to approach you with a question, I would like to ask you, the bhagavān, the tathāgata, the arhat, the perfect and complete Buddha, about certain issues.” ^F231a

Having thus been petitioned, the Bhagavān said to the venerable Rāṣṭrapāla, “Rāṣṭrapāla, ask whatever you like! I will please your mind by clarifying whatever questions you may have.”

Having said these words, the venerable Rāṣṭrapāla asked the Bhagavān, “Bhagavān, how many qualities has a bodhisattva mahāsattva—one who obtains the excellence of all qualities and virtues, who obtains knowledge not depending on others, who gains swift insight, who obtains the ascertainment of eloquence, who obtains illumination, who realizes omniscience, who causes beings to ripen, who eliminates doubt, who eliminates desire, who obtains the ascertainment of omniscience, who is skillful in guiding beings, who acts as he speaks, whose speech is based on genuine intentions, who is skillful in dealing with all sentient beings, who attains the recollection of the Buddha, who asks all questions, who retains all Dharma teachings, and who swiftly obtains omniscience?”

Then the venerable Rāṣṭrapāla uttered the following verses:

“May the supreme one among men, the Victorious One,<br>Give me a precise discourse, an ocean of wisdom,<br>On how to properly ascertain<br>The conduct of a bodhisattva, which arises from truth. {39}

“You are like a body of perfectly pure gold.<br>You are an excellent being who has accumulated supreme merit.<br>You are the refuge, the place of rest, and the protector—<br>Explain to me today the stainless, supreme conduct of a bodhisattva. {40} ^F231b

“How does one obtain the inexhaustible wisdom,<br>The source of retention, of immortality, and of enlightenment?<br>How is the ocean of insight purified,<br>By which means you eradicate people’s doubts? {41}

“While wandering in saṃsāra for many millions of eons,<br>You never grew discouraged—<br>Even when looking at the world, troubled by sufferings—<br>And performed virtue for its sake. {42}

“Please explain the pure buddhafield, the excellent retinue,<br>The supreme lifespan, the excellent field itself,<br>The unsurpassed discourse for the benefit of sentient beings,<br>And the stainless conduct of a bodhisattva. {43}

“You, one who conquers Māra, who purifies wrong views,<br>Who dries up craving, who causes the attainment of liberation,<br>Jewel among sentient beings, please teach the supreme conduct,<br>So that the way of the Dharma will not be forgotten. {44}

“You, one who is endowed with excellent appearance, wealth, and eloquence,<br>Who satisfies the assembly with a gentle voice,<br>And who satisfies the world like a rain cloud—<br>O Sugata, thoroughly reveal the experiential sphere of buddhas. {45}

“You, one who speaks with a pleasant voice like that of the kalaviṅka bird,<br>Who—with a melodious Brahmā voice—destroys vile thoughts,<br>As this Dharma-seeking assembly has gathered,<br>O Lord, satiate us with the taste of the ambrosia. {46}

“I long for supreme, excellent enlightenment,<br>And it is inappropriate to turn away those longing for the Dharma.<br>Now is the time for teaching, O guide;<br>The time has come to proclaim, O excellent jewel. {47}

“I hope for enlightenment, O Sage!<br>The Victorious One truly understands my intentions.<br>I am not deceiving the Victorious One; I am genuinely interested!<br>Please explain the genuine conduct.” {48}

Having thus been petitioned, the Bhagavān said the following to the venerable Rāṣṭrapāla: “You are right, Rāṣṭrapāla! You, Rāṣṭrapāla, have gone forth for the benefit of many living beings. And it is good that you, Rāṣṭrapāla,  have well considered this topic you queried Tathāgata about, for the happiness of many people, for the sake and benefit of gods and men, and so that I will take care of the bodhisattva mahāsattvas present and future. Therefore, Rāṣṭrapāla, listen closely and keep it in your mind! I will explain it to you.” The venerable Rāṣṭrapāla answered, “Very well, Bhagavān!” and as he listened carefully to each of the Bhagavān’s words, the Bhagavān uttered the following words to him: ^F232a

“Rāṣṭrapāla, a bodhisattva mahāsattva endowed with four qualities attains the following types of purity. What are these four? They are making efforts in accordance with beings’ aspirations and highest intent, being impartial toward all sentient beings, meditating on emptiness, and acting just as one speaks. Rāṣṭrapāla, if bodhisattva mahāsattvas are endowed with these four qualities, they will attain purity. This is how it is. In this regard, the following is said:

“Those endowed with infinite wisdom<br>Always possess diligence out of sincere intentions.<br>Having a mind that does not turn away from the path to enlightenment,<br>They are neither deceitful, nor rigid, nor deceptive. {49}

“Seeing the suffering of beings without a protector,<br>Afflicted by birth, sickness, old age, and death,<br>They excellently prepare a Dharma boat<br>To rescue sentient beings from the ocean of existence. {50}

“Gentle ones with equanimity toward all sentient beings<br>Look at beings as if they were their only son;<br>‘I will free all of them’—<br>Such is the intention of supreme persons. {51}

“They always perfectly understand emptiness,<br>That there is neither self nor sentient being,<br>And that the conditioned is like an illusion or a dream.<br>The childish, not being skilled, are confused about these things. {52}

“The skilled ones strive,<br>And fully abide by what they say.<br>The sons of the victorious ones are always tamed, peaceful, without faults,<br>And delighted in the path to enlightenment. {53} ^F232b

“Rāṣṭrapāla, there are four influences that inspire bodhisattvas. What are these four? They are attaining retention, finding a spiritual friend, being receptive to the profound Dharma, and the correct application of completely pure discipline. Raṣṭrapāla, these are the four influences that inspire bodhisattvas. This is how it is. About that, it is said:

“Those of great renown possess retention,<br>By which they behold the supreme Dharma that all buddhas have proclaimed.<br>Because they never lose it, their intellect increases.<br>They possess wisdom, lack attachment, and have mastered all qualities. {54}

“They have found a spiritual friend who causes the branches of enlightenment to grow.<br>The guides show them the supreme path for progress.<br>They do not attend to nefarious friends<br>But turn far away from them like from a scorching fire. {55}

“Once the heroes have heard the profound teaching on emptiness,<br>They never generate any view of a self, a sentient being, or a life force.<br>Their discipline is faultless, and they are endowed with a tame and peaceful mind.<br>Living beings are encouraged to take up the unsurpassable discipline of the Buddha as well. {56}

“Rāṣṭrapāla, these four are the qualities that cause delight in the bodhisattvas dwelling in saṃsāra. What are these four? Rāṣṭrapāla, seeing the Buddha is a quality that causes delight in the bodhisattvas dwelling in saṃsāra. Rāṣṭrapāla, hearing appropriate instructions is a quality that causes delight in the bodhisattvas dwelling in saṃsāra. Rāṣṭrapāla, complete abandonment of possessions is a quality that causes delight in the bodhisattvas dwelling in saṃsāra, and, Rāṣṭrapāla, being receptive to the Dharma of non-apprehension is a quality that causes delight in the bodhisattvas dwelling in saṃsāra. Rāṣṭrapāla, these four are things that cause delight in the bodhisattvas dwelling in saṃsāra.  This is how it is. About this, the following is said: ^F233a

“In every lifetime, they see the perfect buddhas, the supreme ones among men,<br>Who fully illuminate the whole world with their splendor.<br>When seeking supreme, excellent enlightenment for the sake of liberating beings,<br>They abide in delight and devotion; thus, they worship the lord of men, the Victorious One. {57}

“They listen to the peaceful and harmonious Dharma from the guides.<br>Having heard it, they practice it with firm intention, steadily and properly.<br>Hearing the Dharma of non-apprehension, no doubt arises about the fact<br>That all things are without existence [n16] and lack a self. {58}

“They fully renounce all their possessions and acquire nothing.<br>Conscious of having become beggars, their minds are very pleased.<br>They renounce everything—villages, kingdoms, lands, lives, children, and wives—<br>And their minds never waver. {59}

“Rāṣṭrapāla, bodhisattvas should have no concern for four things. What are these four? Rāṣṭrapāla, bodhisattvas should have no concern for living in households. Rāṣṭrapāla, having become renunciants, bodhisattvas should have no concern for gain or honor. Rāṣṭrapāla, bodhisattvas should not be concerned with becoming acquainted with householders. And Rāṣṭrapāla, bodhisattvas should have no concern for their bodies or lives. Rāṣṭrapāla, for these four things bodhisattvas should have no concern. This is how it is. About this, the following is said:

“Having renounced the household with its boundless thicket of faults, [n17] they never have any concern for wealth.<br>These gentle ones, endowed with such qualities, will take delight in the forest, with their senses under control.<br>Wherever they are, they will not draw close to women or men.<br>Like rhinoceroses, they dwell in solitude with a pure, immaculate, stainless intention. {60}

“They do not delight in profit, and do not feel disheartened if they receive nothing.<br>Having few desires, they take pleasure in bare necessities, and they have rejected deceit and hypocrisy.<br>Their minds endowed with diligence for the sake of sentient beings, they are steeped in generosity and spiritual discipline.<br>Having perfected meditative absorption, diligence, and qualities, they strive for buddha wisdom. {61} ^F233b

“Having no concern for either body or life, and having abandoned beloved relatives,<br>They steadily practice the path to enlightenment, with intention firm as a diamond.<br>Even if their bodies were cut into pieces, their minds would not waver.<br>Hoping for omniscience, they steadily exert themselves in diligence. {62}

“Rāṣṭrapāla, there are four things that cause bodhisattvas to be free from distress. What are these four? Rāṣṭrapāla, unimpaired discipline is something that causes bodhisattvas to be free from distress. Rāṣṭrapāla, not giving up life in the forest is something that causes bodhisattvas to be free from distress. Rāṣṭrapāla, following the four noble lineages is something that causes bodhisattvas to be free from distress. And Rāṣṭrapāla, obtaining great erudition is something that causes bodhisattvas to be free from distress. Rāṣṭrapāla, these four are things that cause bodhisattvas to be free from distress. This is how it is. About this, the following is said:

“Inducing desire for this genuine discipline of the buddhas,<br>Protecting flawless discipline like a precious jewel,<br>They do not think, ‘I am endowed with discipline and well restrained.’<br>And they always connect living beings to this very discipline. {63}

“Living in desolate forests at all times,<br>They have no notion of either a self or a life force.<br>Perceiving all forms as being like grass, wood, or stone,<br>They see that there are neither attendants, nor wives, nor possessions. {64}

“They rejoice in the four noble lineages and lack deceit and hypocrisy.<br>Conscientious and resolute in mind, they fully engage in practice.<br>Constantly making effort in erudition and qualities,<br>They strive for the great power of the Sugata’s qualities. {65}

“Having seen the helpless wanderers defeated by birth, aging, and death<br>And oppressed by illness in this prison of existence,<br>They liberate beings from the waves of the ocean of existence<br>By preparing the boat of the peaceful, highest, and excellent Dharma. {66} ^F234a

“There is no other refuge and protector for those<br>Who wander in the conditioned realms of the world. [n18]<br>‘I will completely liberate all those beings’:<br>For this reason, I make this aspiration prayer for the highest enlightenment. {67}

“Rāṣṭrapāla, there are four points that bodhisattvas should know to be states of noble ones. [n19] What are these four? They are obtaining the higher realms—that is to say, meeting with buddhas that appear; serving the gurus—that is to say, tending to them with minds free from worldly concerns; taking delight in remote dwellings—that is to say, without having concern for gain or honor; and obtaining courage—that is to say, being receptive to the profound. Rāṣṭrapāla, bodhisattvas should know that these four points are states of noble ones. This is how it is. About this, the following is said:

“These resolute ones always live in wildwoods and ravines.<br>They never strive for gain in any way.<br>With minds free from desire, they are constantly endowed with courage.<br>They are skilled in the profound Dharma and free from mental elaboration. {68}

“They serve the gurus continuously, uninterruptedly.<br>They act just as they speak.<br>They please an unfathomable number of sugatas.<br>They worship extensively for the sake of the wisdom of the victorious ones. {69}

“The highest realm is for those of noble intent. [n20]<br>They have arrived at the forefront of gods and humans.<br>They always lead sentient beings on the path to enlightenment,<br>And introduce them perfectly to the ten virtues. {70}

“Having heard of the Buddha’s qualities, they become delighted,<br>Thinking, ‘We will reach those shortly.’<br>Having awakened to immaculate, perfect enlightenment,<br>They think, ‘We will liberate billions of sentient beings from limitless suffering.’ {71}

“Rāṣṭrapāla, there are four things that thoroughly purify the enlightened conduct of bodhisattvas. What are these four?  The conduct of a bodhisattva, for those whose minds are without hostility, is as follows: For those who have abandoned hypocrisy, flattery, and extortion of property, it consists in dwelling in the forest. For those who have renounced all possessions, it consists in having no expectations concerning ripening. It further consists in longing for the Dharma day and night, and in not looking for the faults in those who teach the Dharma. Rāṣṭrapāla, these four are the things that thoroughly purify the enlightened conduct of bodhisattvas.” ^F234b

Then, the Bhagavān uttered the following verses:

“Longing for unsurpassable enlightenment,<br>Without rigidity, impurity, or a hostile mind,<br>They do not look for faults in anyone else.<br>They are without deceit, hypocrisy, or conceptualizing minds. {72}

“With the dangerous mentality of a householder, the root of suffering,<br>Some associate with bad people and stray far from the right path.<br>Those who seek liberation abandon it without hesitation;<br>They go forth and make the mountain solitude their home. {73}

“As they confine themselves to various remote forests,<br>They do not rely on making a profit from their knowledge.<br>They give regard neither to their bodies nor health.<br>And, poised like lions, they overcome their enemies. {74}

“Seeking wisdom for the sake of the path to enlightenment,<br>They are content no matter what happens.<br>And without leaving a trace, like a bird,<br>They do not linger anywhere in the world. {75}

“They stay alone like a rhinoceros,<br>And are fearless like a lion.<br>Like a deer they fear sticking to one place.<br>They do not grow proud because of praise. {76}

“Upon having seen that this world has fallen into an abyss,<br>They strive to rescue it.<br>They think, ‘If I carefully practice virtue,<br>I, too, will become a protector of this world.’ {77}

“Striving for this conduct of the supreme ones among men,<br>With a friendly smile, they speak in a pleasant way.<br>Their minds are never troubled by the pleasant or unpleasant.<br>They remain without attachment, like the wind. {78}

“Confident in emptiness and in the absence of characteristics,<br>They conceive all conditioned phenomena to be like illusions.<br>Finding pleasure in peacefulness and self-restraint, and being open-minded,<br>They are always satisfied by the taste of ambrosia. {79} ^F235a

“Doing whatever it takes to accomplish the path to enlightenment,<br>They constantly purify their intent.<br>Seeking retention and eloquence,<br>They desire virtue and endure hundreds of sufferings. {80}

“Whoever exerts themselves will be delighted<br>Upon observing such bodhisattva conduct.<br>Whoever dislikes enlightenment<br>Is of inferior intellect and commits hundreds of faults.” {81}

“Rāṣṭrapāla, there are four pitfalls for bodhisattvas. What are these four? Rāṣṭrapāla, disrespect is a pitfall for bodhisattvas. Rāṣṭrapāla, being ungrateful and dishonest is a pitfall for bodhisattvas. Rāṣṭrapāla, attachment to gain and honor is a pitfall for bodhisattvas. Rāṣṭrapāla, obtaining gain and honor by means of hypocrisy and flattery is a pitfall for bodhisattvas. Rāṣṭrapāla, these four are the pitfalls for bodhisattvas.”

Then, the Bhagavān uttered the following verses:

“They never have respect<br>For the noble gurus or their parents.<br>They are ungrateful and dishonest;<br>Never curbing their behavior, they are deluded. {82}

“Always being attached and clinging to gain,<br>They enjoy cheating and dishonesty.<br>Those who keep moral observances in any way they like<br>Say, ‘There is no one like me!’ {83}

“They are hostile toward each other;<br>Always eager to find faults.<br>They rejoice in farming and trading.<br>They are very far away from having the qualities of an ascetic. {84}

“Not restraining themselves in this manner in the future,<br>They will be very far away from the qualities of a monk’s discipline.<br>By exposing faults out of rancor and jealousy,<br>They will cause the Dharma to decline. {85}

“They are always very far from the path to enlightenment.<br>They are very far from the treasure of the noble ones.<br>Having abandoned the excellent noble path,<br>They will wander in the five realms of existence.” {86}

“Rāṣṭrapāla, there are four things that obstruct the enlightenment of bodhisattvas.  What are these four? Rāṣṭrapāla, laziness is something that obstructs the enlightenment of bodhisattvas. Rāṣṭrapāla, lack of faith is something that obstructs the enlightenment of bodhisattvas. Rāṣṭrapāla, pride is something that obstructs the enlightenment of bodhisattvas. Rāṣṭrapāla, entertaining jealous and avaricious thoughts when others are honored is something that obstructs the enlightenment of bodhisattvas. Rāṣṭrapāla, these four are the things that obstruct the enlightenment of bodhisattvas.” ^F235b

Then, the Bhagavān uttered the following verses:

“Lacking faith, being lazy, and always deluded,<br>They are self-conceited and constantly enraged.<br>When they see a monk who is always patient and exerts himself,<br>They hit him with sticks as he leaves the monastery. {87}

“Feeling envy because others are honored,<br>Their minds are not at rest.<br>Being opportunity seekers and fault finders,<br>They think, ‘I will accuse the one who has faults.’ {88}

“They are far away from this teaching of mine;<br>They hate good qualities and are on their way to lower realms.<br>Having abandoned the teaching of the Victorious One,<br>They go to the extremely horrific, blazing lower realms. {89}

“Having heard about their bad conduct<br>And improper, exceedingly evil way of living here,  [n21]<br>Always practice uninterruptedly the path to enlightenment.<br>Beware—you are sure to be reborn and suffer in the lower realms! {90}

“A buddha, a great sage who benefits the world,<br>Will appear only once in many millions of eons.<br>Now that you have arrived at this excellent and unique moment,<br>Renounce your carelessness if you desire liberation!” {91}

“Rāṣṭrapāla, there are four persons that a bodhisattva should not attend to. Who are these four? Rāṣṭrapāla, a bodhisattva should not attend to a person who is a nefarious friend. Rāṣṭrapāla, a bodhisattva should not attend to a person who entertains an objectifying view. Rāṣṭrapāla, a bodhisattva  should not attend to a person who abandons the true Dharma. And Rāṣṭrapāla, a bodhisattva should not attend to a person who craves material things. Rāṣṭrapāla, these four are the persons that a bodhisattva should not attend to.” ^F236a

Then, the Bhagavān uttered the following verses:

“Those who avoid nefarious friends<br>And turn to virtuous friends<br>Always progress on the path to enlightenment,<br>Like the waxing moon in the sky. {92}

“Those who seek the wisdom of a buddha<br>Always avoid, like a pot of poison,<br>Those who constantly cling to an objectifying view,<br>And who cling to self, vitality, and sustenance. {93}

“Those who desire to awaken to genuine enlightenment<br>Avoid, like a pot of vomit,<br>Those who reject the Dharma of the supreme ones among men,<br>Which is peaceful, passionless, and ambrosia-like. [n22] {94}

“They do not associate with those<br>Who are attached to material things, bowls, and robes,<br>And who always seek to mingle with householders.<br>They avoid them like a pit of fire. {95}

“Those who wish to tame Māra,<br>To turn the excellent, unsurpassed wheel,<br>And to likewise accomplish the benefit of beings<br>Should avoid nefarious friends. {96}

“Those who desire awakening to genuine enlightenment<br>Abandon gain, pleasant and unpleasant,<br>As well as fame, calumny, jealousy, and ego,<br>And constantly seek the wisdom of a buddha.” {97}

“Rāṣṭrapāla, there are four things that result in suffering for bodhisattvas. What are these four? Rāṣṭrapāla, conceit due to knowledge is something that results in suffering for bodhisattvas. Rāṣṭrapāla, a jealous and avaricious mind is something that results in suffering for bodhisattvas. Rāṣṭrapāla, lack of devotion is something that results in suffering for bodhisattvas.  Rāṣṭrapāla, seeking out enjoyments based on knowing and enduring impure things is something that results in suffering for bodhisattvas. Rāṣṭrapāla, these four are the things that result in suffering for bodhisattvas.” ^F236b

Then, the Bhagavān uttered the following verses:

“When the ignorant ones despise<br>Those who uphold the Dharma on earth<br>And are worshiped by all beings,<br>They will encounter limitless suffering. {98}

“These wicked ones—who are always filled with pride<br>And do not bow down to the gurus, the noble beings—<br>Look for riches<br>And always desire impure knowledge. {99}

“Those who have a negative mindset and are directed toward the three lower realms<br>Have no devotion to the Buddha,<br>Dharma, and Saṅgha.<br>They are not devoted to training and practice. {100}

“Having transmigrated from here, the human abode,<br>These unskillful, foolish ones, through their actions,<br>Undergo suffering in the realms of hell, animals,<br>Or hungry ghosts. {101}

“The heroes of men, whose intelligence is a light for the world, [n23]<br>Who terminated suffering,<br>Abandon the paths toward lower realms,<br>And will always follow the path toward enlightenment.” {102}

“Rāṣṭrapāla, four things are fetters for bodhisattvas. What are these four? Despising others is a fetter for bodhisattvas. Engaging in meditation by worldly methods and forming notions about characteristics are a fetter for bodhisattvas. Staying close to someone whose mind clings to everything, who lacks wisdom, and who is careless is a fetter for bodhisattvas. Associating with householders with mental attachments is a fetter for bodhisattvas. Rāṣṭrapāla, these four things are fetters for bodhisattvas.”

Then, the Bhagavān uttered the following verses: ^F237a

“Always despising others,<br>They constantly cultivate worldly meditative absorption.<br>They are fettered by all manner of false views,<br>Like the body of starving elephant stuck in a swamp. {103}

“Those whose fetter it is to associate with householders,<br>Who are always careless and cling to everything,<br>Who have given up wisdom, and whose intelligence is obscured<br>Are fettered by these improper ways of conduct. {104}

“Those who seek liberation from these horrors of suffering—<br>Birth, aging, death, and so on—<br>After casting away their vindictiveness and arrogance,<br>Always persevere on the path to enlightenment. {105}

“Having endured all this endless suffering,<br>Being without attachment to any kind of happiness,<br>And having abandoned all kinsmen, pleasant and unpleasant,<br>These firm ones become buddhas, free from wrongdoing. {106}

“They apply themselves to the six perfections, the spiritual levels, qualities,<br>Powers, faculties, and wisdom.<br>Having always been endowed with all qualities,<br>They become buddhas, free from the web of aging. {107}

“In the past, for an inconceivable number of eons,<br>I always practiced generosity, discipline, and observances,<br>In order to achieve the highest enlightenment for the sake of living beings.<br>Having abandoned my kinsmen, I lived happily. {108}

“I was always joyful in remote forests;<br>I fully purified my intent for the sake of enlightenment.<br>My diligence never waned, even occasionally,<br>As I sought [n24] the wisdom of great beings. {109}

“Once I beheld sentient beings, who are forced to wander<br>In the five realms, this prison of existence,<br>In that very moment I generated vast compassion<br>And accomplished excellent [n25] enlightenment for the sake of the world. [n26] {110}

“I sought supreme enlightenment for many eons<br>And gave up many things: my beloved sons, daughters, and wives,<br>My precious life, and exceedingly abundant lands,<br>Cities, riches, and crops. {111}

“I, the Sage, delighted in patience<br>In pleasant forests abundant with flowers, fruits, and water.<br>Even when King Kali [n27] cut off my hands and legs,<br>My mind did not turn to anger. {112}

“Even when the king pierced me with a sturdy arrow,<br>When I was the sage called Śyāmaka, living in woods and caves,<br>Looking after my old guru,<br>My mind did not generate harmful thoughts. {113} ^F237b

“For the sake of excellent teachings, I jumped off a cliff<br>Without being concerned for my body.<br>For the sake of the causes for supreme enlightenment,<br>I disregarded body and life. {114}

“For the sake of the lives of a tigress and her cubs,<br>I satiated the tigress by sacrificing my body.<br>From the heavens, hosts of gods [n28] proclaimed,<br>‘Excellent, great being with firm effort!’ {115}

“When wandering through former existences, I was<br>A young brahmin who took great delight in generosity;<br>I dried up an ocean for the sake of jewels,<br>And obtained the jewels, with which I delighted sentient beings. {116}

“At the time when I was<br>The widely renowned King Sutasoma,<br>I quickly freed a hundred kings who were about to be killed,<br>Through a truthful deed. {117}

“Having seen a man who was poor and suffering,<br>I gave away my dear body to him. [n29]<br>Thus, he gained wealth; he was made rich by me.<br>At that time, I was King Sarvadada. {118}

“When I was that king,<br>Having seen a pigeon coming for refuge,<br>I even cut flesh from my own body.<br>I gave away my own body; I gave it fearlessly. [n30] {119}

“Formerly, when I was King Kesarin,<br>Even though all sorts of unequaled medicines were produced<br>By the best physicians for me,<br>Sacrificing my own life, I handed them over to others. {120}

“When formerly practicing for the sake of the world,<br>At the time when I was prince Sudaṃṣṭra,<br>I abandoned my loyal wife Madri,<br>As well as my son and daughter, being unattached and unconcerned. {121}

“Formerly, when I was Uttaptavīrya,<br>I endured eighty-four thousand years<br>Of hardship,<br>And I renounced wealth and glory in the past. {122}

“When I was prince Vimalatejas,<br>Setting fire to my body with supreme devotion<br>Before a stūpa containing the relics of the Victorious One,<br>I offered it to those who possess the ten strengths. {123}

“When I was King Candraprabha,<br>Raudrākṣa became enraged;<br>And as he demanded my head,<br>I cut it off and offered it to him. {124} ^F238a

“When I was Puṇyasama,<br>I supplied for the benefit of living beings<br>Copious quantities of various kinds [n31] of medicine<br>At all the arched gates on streets leading into villages and cities. {125}

“While practicing in former existences, [n32]<br>At the time when I was King Śubha,<br>I turned away from thousands of beautiful women,<br>Whose bodies were adorned with gold and pearls. {126}

“Formerly, when I was<br>King Ratnacūḍa,<br>I gave away my most glorious crown,<br>Adorned with flowers, gold, and supreme incenses. {127}

“Formerly, when I was King Dhṛtimān,<br>I even gave away my hands and feet,<br>Which were smooth and tender like soft cotton,<br>And like slender lotus petals. {128}

“When I was the captain Siṃhala,<br>I completely subjugated hundreds of merciless,<br>Extremely frightful, and fierce demonesses,<br>And thus appeased the people on Badara Island. {129}

“When I was Sunetra,<br>I liberated five hundred childish and lustful traders<br>Whose obscured minds<br>Perceived demonesses as young ladies. {130}

“When I was Puṇyaraśmi,<br>I abandoned forty million ladies,<br>With figures like apsarases,<br>And became a renunciate for the sake of the Victorious One’s teaching. {131}

“When I practiced for the benefit of sentient beings<br>As King Kāñcanavarṇa,<br>I gave away my great<br>Webbed fingers. {132}

“When I was King Utpalanetra,<br>I gave away my eyes, colored like beautiful blue lotuses,<br>Charming and delightful,<br>For the sake of the world. {133}

“When I was Keśava, king among physicians,<br>Seeing a woman whose beauty and intelligence had vanished,<br>Afflicted by the separation from her husband,<br>I liberated her out of compassion. {134}

“When I was Sarvadarśin in a former existence,<br>Seeing an ill man stricken by disease,<br>I even gave my own blood,<br>And freed that man from sickness. {135} ^F238b

“When I was the king called Kusuma,<br>I removed a bone from my body<br>And gave the marrow to someone emaciated by disease.<br>I never forsake sentient beings. {136}

“When I was King Arthasiddhi,<br>I gave away my whole treasury,<br>And abandoned my agreeable and pleasant life.<br>I liberated an impoverished man. {137}

“When I was King Āśuketu,<br>Longing for enlightenment for the benefit of the world,<br>I gave away both lotus-like hands,<br>Marked with wheels, without being concerned. {138}

“When I was King Sarvadarśin,<br>I was compassionate and wished to benefit sentient beings.<br>I relinquished even the four continents,<br>Which were crowded with men and women. {139}

“When I was princess Jñānavatī,<br>I cut off my soft, tender, stainless-white thigh<br>With a delighted and joyful mind.<br>I gave my own flesh and blood. {140}

“When I was lady Rūpyāvatī,<br>Having seen a woman tormented by hunger and thirst,<br>I gave away both my lovely,<br>Golden breasts, well rounded [n33] and very youthful. {141}

“When I was King Viśrutaśrī,<br>I abandoned many things that are difficult to abandon:<br>Exceedingly delightful ornaments,<br>Copious jewels, clothes, and chariots. {142}

“When I was prince Kṛtajña,<br>I rescued an ungrateful man from the ocean.<br>He took my eyes to steal my jewels;<br>Yet I was not angry. {143}

“When, in a former existence, I was a lizard who desires ants,<br>Being apprehensive about killing ants,<br>I was not concerned about my excellent body and sacrificed it.<br>At that time my mind was unwavering. {144}

“When I was leading a life as a partridge,<br>I always rejoiced in serving elderly people,<br>And I greatly rejoiced in offering service and showing respect.<br>I harbored no pride or arrogance. {145}

“For the sake of someone who had come for refuge,<br>Cultivating compassion, I sacrificed my body.<br>Even though, as a monkey, a hunter had pierced me with arrows,<br>I did not abandon that man. {146} ^F239a

“When I was under the king’s power,<br>I was mindful of my elderly elephant mother.<br>As my body withered, I lost my appetite, even for delicious food.<br>At that time I, the elephant, became liberated. {147}

“On snowy mountains, difficult to cross, I was king of the bears.<br>Even though the man whom I protected for seven days<br>Surrendered me to a hunter,<br>At that time, I did not feel rage. {148}

“I was an elephant, as white as snow or jasmine,<br>Intent on supreme [n34] enlightenment and seeking the qualities of a buddha.<br>Even though I was pierced by a poisonous arrow,<br>I was not enraged upon surrendering my excellent tusks. {149}

“When I was immersed in loving kindness<br>As a young partridge roaming the woods on Khaṇḍaka Island,<br>A wildfire died out by my merely gazing at it,<br>And the host of gods scattered flowers. {150}

“When I was a deer, I saved someone<br>Who was carried away by the waves of the Ganges.<br>That person led a hunter close to me,<br>Yet I did not become angry at him. {151}

“When I took rebirth as a turtle and rescued five hundred helpless merchants<br>Who were lost in the middle of the sea,<br>I practiced loving kindness,<br>Even though they killed me, driven by hunger. {152}

“Formerly, when I was a fish swimming through the water,<br>While training in the conduct of enlightenment,<br>I sacrificed my body, which was devoured by one hundred thousand animals,<br>In order to benefit sentient beings. {153}

“When I was Prāṇakusaumya,<br>Seeing the world tormented by a hundred types of illness,<br>I transformed my body into medicine<br>And established sentient beings in happiness, healing their diseases. {154}

“When I was a lion, king of wild animals, with strength and compassion,<br>Even though I was pierced by an arrow,<br>I did not become angry.<br>At that time, I generated loving kindness toward my murderer. {155}

“Formerly, when I was the king of horses, white like conches and frost,<br>Living at seaports,<br>I rescued merchants trapped by demonesses<br>By generating love and compassion. {156}

“When practicing the conduct of enlightenment for the sake of living beings,<br>At the time I was a kuṇāla bird.<br>Having relinquished sensual pleasures and numerous wrongdoings,<br>I did not fall under the power of females. {157} ^F239b

“When I was a hare, living in a thicket in the woodlands,<br>I taught good conduct to the assembled hares.<br>For the sake of a sage who was living in his hermitage, oppressed by hunger,<br>I even gave away my body. {158}

“When I was a parrot in a tree full of flowers and fruits,<br>Even though the tree dried up, I did not abandon it.<br>When Śakra saw my gratitude at that time,<br>He made that tree bountiful again. {159}

“When I was the king of the monkeys,<br>The king of the nāgas conquered the country,<br>And having seen a group of monkeys being harmed,<br>I liberated them from their fear of the nāga king. {160}

“Formerly, when I was a parrot,<br>I was seized by a man when I grabbed rice for my parents.<br>He then asked, ‘Parrot, why do you take my rice?<br>Don’t you understand that this destroys these ripe crops?’ {161}

“The parrot said, ‘Good man! Please listen!<br>I did not take your rice out of thievery,<br>But to feed my aged parents.<br>I took the rice for them, out of compassion. {162}

“ ‘In the beginning, when you were sowing the seeds,<br>I heard you saying,<br>“I will give a portion of the rice to all living beings.”<br>Therefore, I am not stealing.’ {163}

“ ‘Very well, parrot! Take the rice as you please! [n35]<br>Such devotion is rare even among humans.<br>It seems you are the human; I am the animal here.<br>How great that you are so disciplined, peaceful, [n36] and well restrained.’ {164}

“Searching for immaculate, excellent enlightenment,<br>When I trained in the past, in a hundred ways difficult to navigate<br>Such as these,<br>My mind was not discouraged. {165}

“Neither within [n37] nor without<br>Is there anything that I have not sacrificed.<br>Thus I practiced discipline, patience, effort,<br>Meditative absorption, skillful means, and insight. {166}

“Flesh and skin, as well as marrow and blood—<br>All this I offered from my own body.<br>When I was roaming remote places and caves,<br>I allowed my body to dry up. {167}

“The victorious ones taught the path of renunciation,<br>Which they adhered to themselves. [n38]<br>I always exerted myself on this path of renunciation.<br>In former lives, I always took this path. {168}

“Such was the vast extent of firm observance<br>I adhered to at the time when I was practicing.<br>Having heard about this and about my conduct,<br>Some monks do not want to hear even a single word of Dharma. {169} ^F240a

“Upon hearing it,<br>They mock this teaching.<br>Defeated by food and sex, [n39]<br>They are constantly overpowered by torpor, and are deceitful like crows. {170}

“Never noble, they are hostile toward the Dharma.<br>They disparage the teachings and are devoid of qualities.<br>When they hear the peaceful Dharma,<br>They say, ‘This was not spoken by the Victorious One.’ {171}

“My teacher was an ocean of knowledge.<br>He was very learned and the best proponent.<br>He refuted their claim<br>That this is by no means the word of the Buddha. {172}

“Moreover, he had an old guru<br>Of unfathomable qualities. [n40]<br>That person also exclaimed, ‘Don’t adopt this!<br>Do not engage with it! This is wrong!’ {173}

“Some say, [n41] ‘Where it is taught that a self does not exist,<br>That there is no life force, and no person of any kind at all,<br>There, the stress of striving to practice moral discipline<br>And to observe one’s vows becomes pointless. {174}

“ ‘If there were a Mahāyāna<br>In which no self, no sentient beings, and no humans existed,<br>It would be useless to exert myself in it,<br>Since it would accept neither a self nor sentient being.’ {175}

“These self-fabrications, [n42] concocted in their own minds<br>By those similar to evil-minded people and wicked heretics–<br>These words would never be taught by the Victorious One<br>In the midst of the assembly of monks.” {176}

“There are monks<br>Who are devoid of embarrassment, shame, and modesty, [n43]<br>Very impudent like crows, agitated and enraged,<br>And consumed by jealousy, pride, and vanity regarding my teaching. {177}

“Waving their hands and feet,<br>They adjust their robes,<br>Draping them like a shawl; [n44] and drunk on beer and haughtiness,<br>They wander through the town and households. {178}

“Having taken up the victory banner of the Buddha, [n45]<br>They perform services for members of households.<br>Having abandoned this teaching—an accumulation of good qualities—<br>They always carry letters with them. [n46] {179}

“They keep cows, horses, donkeys, and cattle,<br>And have male and female servants.<br>Their minds are occupied with agriculture and trade,<br>And they will never be noble. {180} ^F240b

“For them, not a single ignoble word goes unsaid,<br>And there is no action that they are ashamed of.<br>For them, the properties of the stūpa trust and of the Saṅgha<br>Are the same as their personal property. {181}

“Seeing monks rich in qualities,<br>They do not praise them.<br>Having become involved in roguery and lapses in discipline,<br>They nonetheless declare ‘women are terrifying and unbearable.’ [n47] {182}

“A householder is not attracted to pleasures<br>To the same extent as these renunciates!<br>Like householders they end up having wives,<br>Sons, and daughters. {183}

“In households where they are venerated<br>With robes, food, [n48] and riches,<br>They grow enamored of the wives.<br>Never noble, they fall under the influence of afflictions. {184}

“They constantly tell the householders,<br>‘One should not attend to these pleasures,<br>For they cause one to fall into the realms of [n49] animals, hungry ghosts, or hell beings.’<br>However, they themselves are neither disciplined nor calm. {185}

“Just as they themselves are undisciplined,<br>So the assembly of their disciples completely lack discipline.<br>They spend their days and nights<br>Talking about food and sex. {186}

“They pander to the congregation of students,<br>Not to help them attain noble qualities, but to receive their services.<br>They are surrounded by the assembly of their own students,<br>And think, ‘I will always receive offerings from these people!’ {187}

“They tell people,<br>‘I gather them out of compassion;<br>Never do I aim to receive services<br>From the assembly of students.’ {188}

“Many come to them, burdened by illness and leprosy,<br>Suffering from white spots [n50] and repulsive to behold;<br>These monks, never noble,<br>Would ordain anyone who comes to them. {189}

“Bereft of both teaching and precepts,<br>They never strive toward the good qualities of monks;<br>They are neither householders nor monks!<br>They become destitute, like a charnel ground bereft of trees. [n51] {190}

“They have no interest in the prātimokṣa,<br>The trainings, or the Vinaya,<br>But impetuously follow their own will,<br>Like the chief of the elephants released from its iron hook. {191} ^F241a

“They may dwell in the forests,<br>But their thoughts revolve around city life.<br>Always tormented by the fire of their afflictions,<br>Their minds are unsteady. {192}

“Forgetting all the Buddha’s qualities,<br>The trainings, practices, and skillful means,<br>They become filled with vanity, pride, and conceit,<br>And they fall into the terrifying Avīci hell. {193}

“They always take pleasure in stories about kings,<br>And greatly enjoy talking about thieves as well.<br>They take delight in visiting their relatives,<br>And think about them day and night. {194}

“Having forsaken meditative absorption and recitation,<br>They constantly toil in monastic affairs.<br>They scowl and grow attached to their dwelling.<br>Their retinue of disciples is undisciplined as well. {195}

“They say, [n52] ‘I am the custodian of the monastery.<br>Things are done by me according to my agenda.<br>There will be room in this monastery<br>For monks who are in agreement with me.’ {196}

“They do not assemble those<br>Who are endowed with discipline and good qualities,<br>Who uphold the Dharma and exert themselves for the sake of living beings,<br>Always diligent in discipline and restraint. {197}

“They say, ‘This has been selected as my cell,<br>This one is my fellow monk’s,<br>And this one is my companion’s.<br>There is no place for you! Go!’ {198}

“They say, ‘Many monks are residing here.<br>The bedding has all been allotted to them.<br>There is no possibility of acquiring anything.<br>What will you eat here? Leave, monk!’ {199}

“No beds will be<br>Assigned to those good monks. [n53]<br>The rogue monks accumulate riches like householders;<br>They have copious possessions and attendants. {200}

“At a later time, when those sons of mine<br>Are despised on all sides,<br>They will remember my words<br>And reside in remote places and forests. {201} ^F241b

“Alas! Many monks overpowered by wealth<br>And averse to good qualities have appeared.<br>It will not be long before the teachings<br>Of the excellent Victorious One also disappear. {202}

“In future times, those endowed with discipline and qualities<br>Will be constantly despised.<br>Having left behind villages and cities of the kingdom,<br>They will dwell in forests. [n54] {203}

“Those who are divisive, treacherous, fond of quarreling,<br>And devoid of qualities will always be treated with respect.<br>They will be celebrated as teachers of the people<br>And consumed by pride and vanity. {204}

“In this way, my teachings, a treasure of qualities,<br>A mine containing all qualities, exceedingly delightful,<br>Will be destroyed by the defects<br>Of lapses in discipline, of envy, and of vanity. {205}

“In the final period, the teachings will be destroyed,<br>Becoming like a plundered mine of precious jewels,<br>A dried-up lotus pool,<br>And a broken jeweled sacrificial post. {206}

“In its final period, an unbearable time, the noble Dharma<br>Will have completely perished.<br>Undisciplined monks like these<br>Will be the destroyers of my teaching. {207}

“Those who rejoice in adhering to such conduct—<br>How could they exist in higher realms for long?<br>Having died and migrated from here, they will dwell in the realms of hungry ghosts,<br>Animals, and hell beings. {208}

“They will experience limitless suffering,<br>Unbearable and severe, for many hundreds of years.<br>Even if they attain human bodies,<br>Suffering and pain will always be with them. {209}

“Those always engaged in such wrongdoings<br>Will be born blind, deaf, or one-eyed.<br>Their bodies will be constantly plagued by the white spot disease,<br>Disgusting everyone who sees them. {210}

“As for those engaged in such despicable conduct,<br>No one will place their trust in them,<br>No one will believe their words,<br>And they will always be despised. {211}

“Tormented by hundreds of diseases and sufferings,<br>Attacked with weapons, sticks, and clods of earth,<br>And greatly tormented by fear of hunger and thirst,<br>They will always be insulted by others. {212} ^F242a

“Hearing about such limitless sufferings,<br>The gentle ones abandon their wrongdoing.<br>One should always engage in good conduct,<br>Or else one will later have regrets. {213}

“Those who cherish the Buddha, the noble assembly,<br>The trainings, and the qualities of spiritual practice<br>Should abandon their relatives, acquisitions, and reputations<br>And constantly exert themselves accordingly. {214}

“Know [n55] that conditioned things are like an illusion,<br>Perishable, and like a dream,<br>That you will soon be deprived of every pleasure,<br>And that there is nothing permanent here. {215}

“Constantly apply yourself to and strive for<br>The perfections, the bodhisattva levels, and the strengths.<br>So long as you have not attained supreme enlightenment,<br>You should never slacken in your diligence.” {216}

“Rāṣṭrapāla, persons who have boarded the bodhisattva vehicle will generally make the following mistakes: Those without diligence will worship those without diligence; the deceitful will worship the deceitful; and the ignorant ones will think that the ignorant ones [n56] should be respected. They will take pleasure in material things and have numerous attachments. They will envy households, be deceitful, talk nonsense like crows, be hypocritical, and attach importance to relatives. They will enrich themselves by means of praising each other, and they will enter villages to gain profit—not for the sake of assisting beings, or out of kindheartedness. They say, [n57] ‘How can others, those ignorant ones who consider themselves to be knowledgeable, have an opinion about me, the learned one endowed with virtuous qualities?’

“Thus, they make claims to being knowledgeable. They are not—similarly, they do not make any effort. They will be like broken vessels, looking for mistakes in one another, their practice corrupted. They will be ignorant and lazy. Because of their mutual collaboration in reciting broken Dharma together,  they will give little thought to understanding, while fancying their own ideas. They will be unremittingly hostile [n58] and filled with ill will, and they will train others in their teaching by advising them with inappropriate discourse. [n59] They will not be fond of asking questions and will not be interested in listening to the Dharma. Through improper conduct, they will take rebirth in poor households. Renouncing their lives in poor households, they will pretend to take pleasure in this teaching, only for the sake of profit. They will not even confess their faults, let alone realize wisdom. Rejecting the good qualities of the buddhas, they themselves will claim to be ascetics—merely for the sake of public reputation and profit. ^F242b

“Rāṣṭrapāla, I cannot say that such people have even the acceptance that accords with reality, [n60] let alone the wisdom of the buddhas. If even the higher realms are far away for them, how much more so is enlightenment?

“Rāṣṭrapāla, I speak of eight things that cause obstacles to enlightenment for persons like those. What are these eight? Taking rebirth in the three lower realms, taking rebirth among people of the borderlands, taking rebirth in poor households, taking rebirth in low-caste families, being ugly and blind, associating with nefarious friends, having many diseases, and dying without relinquishing distress. Rāṣṭrapāla, I say that these eight things cause obstacles to enlightenment. Why is that?

“Rāṣṭrapāla, I do not speak of the enlightenment of someone who acknowledges it only verbally. I do not speak of the completely pure conduct of someone who is hypocritical. I do not speak of the conduct conducive to enlightenment of someone who is deceitful. I do not speak of buddha worship of someone who attaches importance to worldly concerns. I do not speak of the completely pure insight of someone who is conceited. I do not speak of the removal of doubt of someone who has faulty knowledge. I do not speak of the completely pure intention of someone who is avaricious. I do not speak of retention in the case of someone who has little diligence. I do not speak of the attainment of the higher realms of someone who does not seek pure qualities.  I do not speak of the completely pure body of someone who envies households. ^F243a

“I do not speak of meeting the Buddha in the case of someone whose noble path [n61] is contrived. I do not speak of the complete purity of speech of someone who is attached to a household. I do not speak of the complete purity of mind in the case of someone who is not respectful. I do not speak of the longing for the Dharma in the case of someone who is unrestrained. I do not speak of the quest for the Dharma in the case of someone who is overly concerned with body and life. Rāṣṭrapāla, I do not reproach even the six heretical teachers to the extent that I reproach those fools. Why is that? They speak in one way and act another. They deceive the whole world, including the gods.”

Then, the Bhagavān uttered the following verses:

“Uncontrolled, wild, arrogant,<br>Disrespectful, proud, overly attached,<br>Overpowered by afflictions, cold hearted, and preoccupied with wealth— [n62]<br>Such people are very far away from enlightenment. {217}

“For someone who is overpowered by attachment, laziness increases;<br>For someone who is overpowered by laziness, faith is destroyed;<br>For someone whose faith is lost, discipline is destroyed;<br>And for someone who has lapsed in discipline, the chance of [n63] the higher realms is destroyed. {218}

“Since such people have become poor, they become renunciants.<br>Having received adulation, they then escape poverty.<br>It is as if they have thrown away a load of gold<br>And taken up a load of grain. {219}

“Bent on making profits, they go into the forest;<br>Having gone there, they only seek garments and frequent their relatives.<br>Having abandoned the excellences of superknowledge, knowledge, and eloquence,<br>They repeatedly latch on to their relatives. {220}

“Due to their pride, such deluded ones [n64] choose<br>The lower realms, a condition among the unfortunate states,<br>Poverty, birth into a low-caste family,<br>Blindness, ugliness, and weakness. {221}

“Being in a condition devoid of character and proper conduct,<br>Their mindfulness is destroyed by recklessness and profiteering. [n65]<br>They fall into a great and terrifying abyss<br>From which there is no liberation for millions of eons. {222}

“If enlightenment were possible through profit-making in this life,<br>Even the likes of [n66] Devadatta would attain enlightenment.<br>As birds are swept downward by violent winds,<br>So are the improper ones by attachment. {223} ^F243b

“Their merit is destroyed, they desire others’ wives,<br>Their discipline is impure, and they are morally bankrupt.<br>Those who have not obtained wisdom through the aspiration for enlightenment<br>Are not inclined toward the teaching, as one is toward wood at a cremation ground. {224}

“Bodhisattvas [n67] aiming for enlightenment seek the buddha qualities,<br>But they do not dwell like someone in possession of liberation.<br>Just like a monkey stuck in an adhesive trap,<br>Such is the enlightenment of those overcome by pride. {225}

“Because I was aiming for enlightenment, for the sake of a single word of the Dharma<br>I even gave away my own precious life.<br>Because those lacking diligence have abandoned this Dharma,<br>They are aimless and will veer away from the teaching. {226}

“Formerly, for the sake of the well-explained doctrine<br>I jumped into a deep abyss, blazing with flames.<br>After hearing it, I abided by it persistently, having abandoned<br>All pleasant and unpleasant things. {227}

“Listening to the manifold teachings, rich in good qualities,<br>Bodhisattvas never long for them.<br>How, then, could there be enlightenment for those who long for what is opposed to the Dharma,<br>Like a blind person for whom there is no illuminated road?” {228}

“Formerly, Rāṣṭrapāla, at a time in the past, truly innumerable, innumerable, very long, inconceivable, incomparable, measureless, immeasurable eons ago, there appeared in the world the illustrious buddha known as Siddhārthabuddhi. He was a tathāgata, an arhat, a perfect and complete buddha endowed with perfect knowledge and conduct, a sugata, knower of the world, an unsurpassed guide of those to be trained, a teacher of gods and men. At that time, Rāṣṭrapāla, there was a king called Arciṣmān. Rāṣṭrapāla, King Arciṣmān ruled over a kingdom in Jambudvīpa extending over sixteen thousand yojanas. At that time, Rāṣṭrapāla, there were twenty thousand cities and ten billion households in Jambudvīpa.

“Rāṣṭrapāla, King Arciṣmān had a city named Ratnaprabhāsa, the capital where he lived. From east to west it was twelve yojanas in length, and from south to north it was seven yojanas in breadth. It was decorated with seven successive concentric outer walls made of the seven precious substances  and ornamented with painted checkered patterns. At that time, the beings’ lifespans reached a hundred million years. ^F244a

“Rāṣṭrapāla, king Arciṣmān had a son called Puṇyaraśmi, who had a beautiful body and was handsome, pleasant to look at, and magnificently endowed with a most excellent, splendid complexion. As soon as he was born, a thousand treasuries of the seven precious substances manifested. In the king’s court, too, manifested a treasury of seven precious substances that was about the height of ten men. Rāṣṭrapāla, as soon as young prince Puṇyaraśmi was born, all the beings of Jambudvīpa rejoiced. The prisoners were released from jail.

“Rāṣṭrapāla, young prince Puṇyaraśmi mastered all the worldly arts, as many as there are, in seven days. Then, Rāṣṭrapāla, in the middle of the night, the gods of the Śuddhāvāsa realm roused young prince Puṇyaraśmi saying, ‘Young prince, be conscientious! Become skilled in the thorough analysis of impermanence! Young prince, the life of men is not long. You will have to depart from this life. Behold the other world with fear, and do not act in contradiction with your duties.’ At that time, the gods also uttered the following verses:

“ ‘Young prince, beware of carelessness!<br>Beware of coming under the power of carelessness!<br>The sugatas praise the conscientious ones;<br>The sugatas scold the careless ones. {229}

“ ‘The gentle ones here who are conscientious,<br>Who enjoy goodness and restraint, are not avaricious,<br>And are full of compassion and loving kindness toward all sentient beings,<br>Will before long become the supreme ones among men. {230}

“ ‘The sugatas of the past,<br>The present, and the future<br>Are all exalted due to their virtues.<br>They remain solely on the path of conscientiousness. {231} ^F244b

“ ‘For tens of millions of eons, they gave away<br>Food, drink, clothing, and meals,<br>As well as gold, silver, jewels, pearls, and ornaments,<br>Aiming for excellent enlightenment. {232}

“ ‘As they have joyfully given up their feet, hands, ears, and noses<br>To those asking for them,<br>With their intent perfected in terms of the qualities of enlightenment,<br>They will soon become the supreme ones among men. {233}

“ ‘Abandon the enjoyments of the kingdom, your beloved women,<br>And even all possessions,<br>And live in the forest, without desire,<br>Because conditioned things are like a spectacle on a stage, or an illusion. {234}

“ ‘Life is unstable and always changing,<br>Fragile like a clay pot.<br>Like something borrowed and unenduring,<br>Young prince, it is without permanence and unpleasant. {235}

“ ‘In this life, when you fall into the lower realms,<br>Even your father and mother won’t be able to hold on to you.<br>Whatever virtuous or nonvirtuous deeds are done by men<br>Will always follow them at the end. {236}

“ ‘For many oceans of eons, you and beings have killed each other<br>Because of desire and without reason.<br>You have not helped anyone.<br>The hardship you are engaged in is useless. {237}

“ ‘Now that you seek the incomparable peace of enlightenment<br>For the benefit of the world, the highest spiritual level,<br>And now that your marrow, flesh, and skin are drying up,<br>Should you not exert yourself? {238}

“ ‘Rare is the appearance of a sugata;<br>Extremely rare is it to listen to the peaceful Dharma.<br>Zealously clearing away Māra’s armies,<br>You will attain buddha wisdom before long. {239}

“ ‘Always attend to virtuous friends,<br>And avoid nefarious friends.<br>Virtuous friends [n68] always lead you to the virtuous path;<br>They always cause you to shun the wrong path. {240}

“ ‘Perfectly develop resolute effort;<br>Abandon the desire for body and life.<br>With a firm intention whose nature is like a vajra,<br>Devote yourself to this path of the Buddha. {241}

“ ‘When all the former supreme ones among men, the illuminators,<br>Were seeking the best, unexcelled state,<br>They took pleasure in the experience of the forest.<br>You too should follow in their footsteps. {242} ^F245a

“ ‘Always take pleasure in living in the forest;<br>Abandon father, mother, sons, dear friends,<br>And all others close to your heart,<br>Without craving for body and life.<br>Seek now the vast wisdom of the Sugata,<br>The supreme, unsurpassable message.’ [n69] {243}

“Rāṣṭrapāla, from then on, for ten years the young prince Puṇyaraśmi was never overpowered by drowsiness or torpor. He never laughed, never played, never enjoyed himself, and never engaged in sports. He never went to the pleasure groves, he was never excited to meet friends, and he never took pleasure in music. He never desired kingship, wealth, houses, or cities. Thus, he had no concern for anything, and was engaged in solitary reflection. Devoting himself to secluded contemplation, he contemplated, in a condition of utter helplessness, that the world is without essence and subject to destruction. He thought about meeting with unpleasant things and being separated from pleasant things, about how the childish are deceived, and that there is no enjoyment of pleasure in saṃsāra. He thought about the deceptive character of the enjoyments of kingship, and how one cannot be content with the enjoyments of existence. And he reflected upon ordinary persons, constantly in disagreement, thinking, ‘As I am in the middle of childish ones, who are not diligent, I should spend my time in silence.’ Alone, he stayed in silence. Pondering conscientiously, he abided alone, aloof from pleasurable things.

“Then, Rāṣṭrapāla, King Arciṣmān had a city built elsewhere, called Ratipradhāna, for the young prince’s enjoyment. He constructed seven hundred streets running north and seven hundred running south. It was encircled on all sides by seven successive concentric outer walls made of the seven precious substances, adorned with nets of small bells, nets of pearls, and jeweled pillars. One thousand jeweled pillars were erected at the arched gates to all the streets. Sixty thousand strings of jewels were fastened to all the jeweled pillars. In the four directions, ten million garlands of cymbals were tied to all the strings of jewels. [n70] When they were stirred into motion by the wind, a sound arose like the pleasant melody of hundreds of thousands of musical instruments. At each arched gate to the streets were stationed five hundred girls, skilled in song and dance, in the prime of their lives, appropriate for the enjoyment of all the people. ^F245b

“To all of them King Arciṣmān gave this command: ‘Day and night you should truly please sentient beings who approach from the four directions with song, dance, and the sound of music. By every means, you should generate nothing but thoughts that please the young prince. Do not speak about anything else. Do not say anything unpleasant to sentient beings.

“ ‘At the arched gates leading to all the streets, food should be offered to those who long for food, drink to those who long for drink, and vehicles to those who long for vehicles. In addition to these things, they should be given all manner of clothing, fragrant substances, garlands, ointments, beds, cushions for their backs, basic necessities, unrefined gold, silver, jewels, pearls, beryl, shells, crystals, coral, gold and silver coins, elephants, horses, chariots, oxen, cattle, and adornments.’ This abundance of precious objects was displayed to be enjoyed by everyone.

“At that time, in the center of the city, a house was built for the young prince’s enjoyment. It was about one yojana in circumference, checkered with the seven precious substances, and adorned with hundreds of arched gates. On the inside a court was built, where forty million cushions were arranged for the enjoyment of the young prince. In the middle of that, a pleasure grove was created, filled with all sorts of flowering trees, fruit trees, and jeweled trees.  Rāṣṭrapāla, in the midst of this pleasure grove, a pond made of various kinds of precious substances was constructed. It was endowed with steps made of the four kinds of precious substances, namely, gold, silver, beryl, and crystal. One hundred eight lions’ mouths were erected, through which scented water streamed into the pond. Also, through one hundred eight lions’ mouths, water flowed out of that pond. ^F246a

“Blue, red, pink, and white lotuses bloomed constantly. The pond [n71] was completely encircled by jeweled trees and enriched by everlasting flowering and fruit trees. On the banks of this pond were planted eight hundred jeweled trees, and on all these jeweled trees were fastened strings of jewels. To each of these were fastened ten million garlands of cymbals. When they were stirred into motion by the wind, a sound arose like the pleasant melody of hundreds of thousands of musical instruments. To ensure that the young prince’s body was touched by neither earth nor dust, [n72] a canopy of jewels was spread out above this pond.

“At that time forty million thrones, made of seven precious substances, were arranged in this court, and on each of the thrones were spread five hundred pieces of calico fabric.

“A throne was erected, fashioned out of the seven precious substances, about the height of seven men, where young prince Puṇyaraśmi was seated. In the middle of this, eight hundred and seventy million pieces of calico fabric were spread out. Everywhere at the base of the throne incense pots of agarwood perfumed the air. Three times per day and three times per night, flowers were scattered. The throne [n73] was decorated with a golden covering. It was draped with golden lotuses. It was arrayed with nets of pearls and jewels, which shone with the splendor of precious jewels. Eighty thousand jeweled canopies were suspended. On every jeweled tree, a hundred banners were suspended. Throughout this pleasure grove, nine million nine hundred thousand lights of precious strings of jewels measuring about one yojana were placed. The whole world  was illuminated by their splendor. ^F246b

“Rāṣṭrapāla, in this pleasure grove lived flocks of birds that spoke in human tongues, namely, parrots, myna birds, cranes, cuckoos, peacocks, geese, wild geese, kuṇāla birds, kalaviṅka birds, and partridges. When they spoke and sang, a sound came forth that satisfied the gods of the pleasure grove. For the young prince’s enjoyment, meals of five hundred flavors, replete with all sorts of tastes, were continuously arrayed. At that time, youths between the ages of sixteen and twenty years were rounded up from all the cities and led to this grove. Eight hundred million people, all of whom were learned in arts and crafts, in the worldly pleasures, and in acts of service, were led to this city.

“Ten million girls were offered to him by his parents, ten million by his relatives, ten million by people of the city, and ten million by all the kings. All of them had exclusively gorgeous bodies, ravishing and delightful. Moreover, they were all sixteen years of age, and skilled in singing, playing instruments, dancing, entertaining, and the ways of approaching men. They were all upright, youthful, and charming, with soft curves. They were honest in speech, had smiling faces and skillful behavior, and were skilled in all arts. They were neither too tall nor too short, neither too thin nor too fat, and neither too fair nor too dark. Their breaths carried the scent of lotus flowers, and from their bodies emanated the fragrance of sandalwood. They appeared as brilliant as divine maidens, looking extremely attractive and singing melodious songs. Amidst them was seated young prince Puṇyaraśmi.

“With regard to these sounds of song and music, he gave rise to the thought, ‘Alas!  This great assembly is not friendly toward me and has appeared in order to destroy my virtuous qualities. I will not concern myself with them!’ At that time, just like a man who is about to be killed and is not surprised upon seeing his executioner, the young prince Puṇyaraśmi was not surprised when he saw those women. And he was not surprised to see his female companions in the city. [n74] During those ten years he thought, ‘I will become liberated from such an inhospitable assembly.’ And he pondered, ‘When will I lead a conscientious life, through which I can become liberated?’ Apart from generating such thoughts, he never grasped for the characteristic signs of form. ^F247a

“Likewise, he did not grasp for the characteristics of sound, smell, taste, or touch.

“Then those girls said, ‘Your majesty, King Arciṣmān, the young prince does not engage in play, he does not enjoy himself, and he refuses to be courted.’

“Then, Rāṣṭrapāla, King Arciṣmān, together with eighty thousand kings, went to young prince Puṇyaraśmi. Having arrived there, with tears running down his cheeks, his body trembled. Grieving, he tumbled to the ground. When he arose, he uttered the following verses to young prince Puṇyaraśmi:

“ ‘My only son, excellent jewel, look at me and speak!<br>Tormented by sorrow at home, I have fallen to the ground.<br>Tell me quickly, who has displeased you?<br>I shall punish them severely here and now. {244}

“ ‘Look at this city, pleasing like those of the gods,<br>That I have built [n75] here for your sake.<br>Tell me quickly which part of it is insufficient,<br>And I will present you today with riches like those of Śakra. {245}

“ ‘Look at this multitude of women like heavenly maidens,<br>With beautiful, lotus-like eyes, now lamenting and afflicted with grief.<br>Overcome your worries and frolic with them today!<br>Why do you sit here glumly like someone pierced by a thorn? {246} ^F247b

“ ‘They are skilled in the arts of pleasure and have melodious voices.<br>They know precisely the right times for singing and instruments.<br>The time for pleasing you has come, so do not mourn!<br>Why are you so downcast like a wilting lotus? {247}

“ ‘There is a pleasure grove with flowers, fruits, and tree branches full of leaves,<br>Variegated and looming high like the Citraratha Grove of the gods.<br>Think about it—your youth has just begun.<br>Since this is the time for enjoyment, enjoy yourself here and now, my son! {248}

“ ‘Your pond for bathing resembles even that of the gods.<br>It is blanketed with a forest of blue and pink lotuses.<br>The lotuses are also adorned with exquisite, intoxicated bees.<br>Think about that, my son—who would not take pleasure in it? {249}

“ ‘Geese, peacocks, parrots, cuckoos, myna birds,<br>Kalaviṅka birds, partridges, and kuṇāla birds<br>All have melodious voices like those on Mount Gandharvamādana [n76] near the Himālayas—<br>Hearing them, what man would not find pleasure in them? {250}

“ ‘Mansions are endowed with nets of pearls and embedded with jewels.<br>They are adorned with gold and beryl like Indra’s [n77] Palace of Victory.<br>Excellent jeweled thrones are adorned<br>With golden bells, small bells, and garlands of cymbals that make beautiful sounds. {251}

“ ‘A supremely sonorous and continuous sound announces<br>That for your sake, generous gifts are spread out on the streets.<br>A thousand girls sing sublime, enchanting songs,<br>Resounding like those of the heavenly maidens in the pleasure groves.<br>My son, why do you not find pleasure through distraction<br>In this pleasant haven resembling the godly realms? [n78] {252}

“ ‘Young prince, your playmates resemble the sons of gods.<br>Go play with those boys!<br>Your parents are choked with tears.<br>Don’t you feel any anguish or compassion for them?’ {253}

“Disgusted with the conditioned, and not seeking the enjoyments of lust,<br>The one who had accrued good qualities and perceived the faults of existence said,<br>‘Upon seeing these living beings who have entered the cage of saṃsāra,<br>I aim for liberation—please listen, father! {254}

“ ‘Your majesty, no one has done anything unpleasant to me.<br>I have no longing for sensory pleasures now.<br>Making one fall into the abyss of defiling emotions and lower destinies,<br>All these alluring beauties are like enemies—I am not fond of them. {255} ^F248a

“ ‘Unwise, foolish people take pleasure in these women.<br>Bound by the charms of Māra’s noose, they are great pitfalls.<br>Accordingly, they are always condemned by noble beings.<br>Why should I attend to a mind that is the root of suffering in the hells and lower realms? {256}

“ ‘These women are only enjoyable on the surface.<br>I do not set my heart on these impure contraptions of bones,<br>Leaking filth as they do, such as blood, urine, and excrement.<br>How could I enjoy something that clearly belongs in the charnel grounds? {257}

“ “I will listen to neither song nor the sound of dance and music.<br>These joys are like dreams and confuse the unwise.<br>The unwise, attached to conceptions, will be destroyed here.<br>Why should I become enslaved by afflictions like these fools? {258}

“ ‘Come winter, all these trees and creepers<br>Will be like wasteland shrubs, not enjoyable at all.<br>This impermanence destroys all glory.<br>Out of ignorance, I become reckless in this unstable life. {259}

“ ‘The thirst of the mind cannot be quenched, as is the case when drinking ocean water.<br>By enjoying things with ever-increasing craving, [n79] one always wants more.<br>Looking at beings who kill each other out of desire,<br>I am as unwavering as Mount Meru in the wind. {260}

“ ‘You, king, my father, as well as my siblings,<br>My relatives, and my wife cannot protect me from the lower realms.<br>We are all like dew drops on tips of grass.<br>May we not fall prey to our desirous minds; may we be careful. {261}

“ ‘O lord of men, to hell with youth, which is impermanent.<br>To hell with life, which passes quickly like a mountain river.<br>To hell with the conditioned, which is transient like a thundercloud.<br>Unwholesome, O king, is attachment to objects of desire, for the wise in the three worlds. {262}

“ ‘The gods urged me, “Be careful!<br>A bodhisattva is not attached to the sense objects.”<br>I will be a buddha for the benefit of, and out of compassion for, the world.<br>O king, there is no enlightenment for one who acts recklessly. {263} ^F248b

“ ‘O king, one who is afflicted by desire [n80] is a slave of the mind.<br>Taking delight in squandering merit, one turns away from the higher realms.<br>Never interact with someone seething with violence.<br>Like a bird put in a cage, how will one find relief? {264}

“ ‘The elements are like snakes, the aggregates like executioners,<br>And the contaminated mind resembles a vacant, worthless city.<br>Here, O king, if the body, which is like a flower blooming on a poisonous stalk,<br>Is swept away by the river, how could I take pleasure in it? {265}

“ ‘Having observed this world, which has fallen into bad destinies,<br>This brightly blazing place, vast as space—<br>In order to set it free here and now, O king,<br>And to wake those asleep and cure those who are sick, {266}

“ ‘In order to thoroughly extract the thorn and turn the world away from wrong paths,<br>I will quickly prepare the boat of the peaceful Dharma.<br>Having taught liberation for those bound in the trichiliocosm,<br>I will satisfy those who hungry for the well-explained doctrine for a long time. {267}

“ ‘I will rescue those who have descended along the paths of lower realms.<br>I will give my eyes to the blind and dry up the creeper of craving.<br>I will be a light of insight, a lamp for the sake of supreme liberation,<br>By which the three worlds are seen as resembling an actor’s theater. [n81] {268}

“ ‘With lightning garlands of deep insight, clouds of loving kindness,<br>The thunder of friendliness, compassion, and the perfections for sentient beings’ well-being,<br>And the veil of soothing rain of the branches of enlightenment,<br>I will cool the world, which has been tormented for a long time. {269}

“ ‘Recalling this, O king, I am here<br>Without aspirations for any conditioned, desirable things.<br>I will practice here, aiming at enlightenment for the welfare of beings.<br>It is certain that I will not take pleasure in worldly existence. {270}

“ ‘Who, O king, would knowingly dwell here among enemies?<br>Which intelligent person would travel on a fearful path?<br>Who with open eyes would fall into this abyss?<br>Knowing the path to enlightenment, who would become reckless? {271}

“ ‘All beings go with the stream, but I go against it.<br>By mere words, O king, one cannot achieve enlightenment.<br>I would even leap into the ocean from Mount Meru’s peak,<br>But I would never delight in these desirable things here. {272} ^F249a

“ ‘Go swiftly, O excellent king, together with your kinsmen,<br>And bestow all the pleasures of your kingdom upon everyone.<br>Let them proceed to take whatever they wish.<br>I will not grow reckless, father, craving millions [n82] of kingdoms. {273}

“ ‘Amidst a crowd of women, one cannot achieve enlightenment,<br>This accomplishment of bliss, peace, and the unexcelled state.<br>Because the Victorious One attained enlightenment by rejoicing in the forest,<br>I, too, shall go and stay on mountains and in forests.’ {274}

“Then, Rāṣṭrapāla, while young prince Puṇyaraśmi was residing in his court, he took a stroll in the company of these distraught women, and he abided by three ways of conduct. What are these three? They are giving up drowsiness and torpor while standing, going, and sitting. Having ascended to the roof of the palace, he heard the gods of the Śuddhāvāsa realm on the eighth floor extensively praising the Buddha, Dharma, and Saṅgha as they were flying through the air. Having heard this, Rāṣṭrapāla, young prince Puṇyaraśmi, his hairs bristling with euphoria, shed tears and, overwhelmed by excitement [n83] and with palms joined together, uttered the following verse to the gods:

“ ‘O gods, you have generated compassion for me, a suffering man;<br>And if it does not disturb you, I will ask you a short question:<br>Of whose good qualities do you speak as you fly through the air here?<br>Having heard your words, my mind is now delighted.’ {275}

“At this point, Rāṣṭrapāla, the gods uttered the following verse to young prince Puṇyaraśmi:

“ ‘O prince, have you not heard of the Buddha<br>Called Siddhārthabuddhi, the refuge for those without refuge?<br>He is skilled in conduct, endowed with highest merit, insight, and qualities.<br>His assembly consists of countless meditators.’ {276} ^F249b

“Puṇyaraśmi replied:

“ ‘May I, too, behold [n84] the Victorious One!<br>Please tell me everything: What are his body and complexion like?<br>How should one practice to become a protector for all sentient beings?<br>What is the conduct of a bodhisattva like?’ {277}

“The gods then uttered the following verses to young prince Puṇyaraśmi:

“ ‘His uṣṇīṣa is as beautiful as gold on a mountain summit.<br>His hair, curling to the right, is soft and lovely.<br>The hair tuft between his eyebrows, blazing like the sun in the sky,<br>Is completely pure like a crystal jewel, and it curls to the right. {278}

“ ‘His pure eyes are like a swarm of bees and blue lotuses.<br>He, the lord of men—endowed with jaws of a lion and lips like the self-arisen bimba fruit—<br>Radiates myriad boundless light rays simultaneously to all sentient beings,<br>And thus illuminates the trichiliocosm and dries up the lower realms. {279}

“ ‘His forty teeth, pure like glittering white snow or silver,<br>Are straight, without gaps, and marvelously well arranged.<br>The supreme and excellent Victorious One has four eye teeth,<br>And his large tongue could cover his face. {280}

“ ‘The speech of the Victorious One is majestic like the highest mountain.<br>It is exceptionally satisfying, coherent, and honest.<br>His Brahmā voice is melodious, like a thousand cymbals;<br>It alleviates doubts and satisfies all longings. {281}

“ ‘The speech of the Victorious One delights like a chorus of the gods.<br>All its manifold qualities are complete, in accord with the branches of enlightenment.<br>It is a charming hundred-thousand-threaded Dharma garland,<br>Resounding like delightful cymbals and pleasing gods and nāgas. {282}

“ ‘It has the melodies of kinnaras and the calls of kalaviṅka birds, cuckoos, wild geese, peacocks, geese, and kuṇāla birds—<br>A symphony of sounds like a melodious Brahmā voice.<br>The melodies of kinnaras are guileless and faultless,<br>Fostering the realization of all aims. {283}

“ ‘Smooth like a marvelous crystal, delighting the learned ones,<br>Inspiring, calming, enlightening, [n85] and pleasing; {284}<br>In accord with the highest conduct, and satisfying questions:<br>These are the qualities of the Dharma Lord’s speech. ^F250a

“ ‘The teacher’s neck is lovely like a conch, his shoulders and head are round,<br>His arms are long like staves, he has the seven prominent body parts. [n86]<br>His hands are elegant, his fingers are elongated and round.<br>The Victorious One’s body is the color of perfectly pure gold. {285}

“ ‘His bodily hair curls to the right, each hair standing straight.<br>His navel is deep, and his secret part sheathed like that of a horse.<br>The Self-Realized One has thighs like elephants’ trunks and the calves of an antelope.<br>The soles of his feet are beautifully adorned, marked with auspicious wheels. [n87] {286}

“ ‘His gait [n88] is like that of the chief of elephants, of a courageous lion,<br>And like that of a majestic [n89] bull. He is mighty like the nāga Indrayaṣṭi.<br>A rain of flowers from the sky forms a canopy of flowers, following him.<br>These are his marvelous qualities. {287}

“ ‘The Victorious One is entirely unstained by gain and loss,<br>Pleasure and suffering, fame and disgrace, and praise and blame,<br>Like a lotus unstained by water.<br>There is no sentient being equal to this lion among men.’ {288}

“Rāṣṭrapāla, once young prince Puṇyaraśmi had heard this praise of the Buddha, Dharma, and Saṅgha, he was contented, happy, delighted, and overjoyed. Happiness and mental pleasure were born in him.

“Then, Rāṣṭrapāla, young prince Puṇyaraśmi thought the following:

“ ‘What is the Buddha, the Bhagavān, like? What is the excellent Saṅgha like? What is the Dharma that he revealed like? What are his excellent disciples like, so that they realize how saṃsāra is frightful and antagonistic; how in saṃsāra, childish ordinary beings are ungrateful; how the view that the aggregates are a person is frightful; how household life has many drawbacks; how passions have many faults; how recklessness is condemned by the learned ones; how the darkness of ignorance brings delusion;  how formative predispositions are difficult to realize; how the mind is difficult to tame; how name and form are profound; how the six sense fields are not reliable; how, when sensory contact is not thoroughly understood, suffering will ripen; how sensations have all sorts of drawbacks; how becoming is a true fetter; [n90] how it is difficult to escape from appropriation; how craving for worldly existence is not noble; how, when there is worldly existence, the continuity of birth is difficult to terminate; how old age [n91] brings change; how sickness causes destruction; how one is not protected after death; how the transition stage is abhorrent; how the emergence into existence has many drawbacks; and how the teaching of the Tathāgata is delightful. ^F250b

“ ‘These realizations are not possible for those who are slaves to desire, impaired by afflictions, deluded, and hardhearted, who take pleasure in recklessness, who live among fools, who have improper thoughts and thoughts attached to saṃsāra, and who reside among pernicious men. And if they are not even able to fully practice the path to higher realms, how could they realize unsurpassable, completely perfect enlightenment?’

“He thought, ‘If I left through the door, the assembly of my kinsmen would prevent me. Therefore, I will jump from the upper roof of the palace.’

“At this point, Rāṣṭrapāla, young prince Puṇyaraśmi, facing the direction of the Bhagavān, the Tathāgata Siddhārthabuddhi, jumped from the palace, saying, ‘If the Tathāgata knows everything and sees everything, may he pay heed to me.’

“Just then, Rāṣṭrapāla, the tathāgata, arhat, perfect and complete Buddha  Siddhārthabuddhi extended his right hand and issued forth light, which touched Puṇyaraśmi. From that light, a hundred-thousand-petaled lotus the size of a chariot wheel emerged. From that lotus, a hundred thousand light rays shone forth, and there was a great brightness. Young prince Puṇyaraśmi was enveloped by this brightness. Then, Rāṣṭrapāla, young prince Puṇyaraśmi, resting on the lotus, faced the direction of the bhagavān, tathāgata, arhat, perfect and complete Buddha Siddhārthabuddhi, bowed with joined palms, and uttered three times, ‘I pay homage to the Buddha.’ At this point, Rāṣṭrapāla, the Tathāgata Siddhārthabuddhi withdrew the light, and the prince fell at the feet of the tathāgata—just as a tree falls when it is chopped down—and praised the tathāgata a hundred thousand times. ^F251a

“Then, Rāṣṭrapāla, young prince Puṇyaraśmi uttered the following verses to the Bhagavān, Tathāgata Siddhārthabuddhi:

“ ‘For a long time I was sick; for a long time I was blind.<br>Now after great hardship, I have found you, king of physicians.<br>Show me, O protector, how I can be firm<br>And successful in the teaching of the Sugata. {289}

“ ‘The understanding of conscientiousness, O guide,<br>I have received at night from the gods in the sky.<br>Having understood it, I became distraught, thinking,<br>“How can people still be reckless?” [n92] {290}

“ ‘Now be the guide for one who has lost his way;<br>Now be the eyes for one who is blind from birth.<br>Source of faith, compassionate healer,<br>Pull me out of this great abyss! {291}

“ ‘Please favor me, a person in poverty;<br>Now, O protector, free me, a person in chains.<br>Please cut through my doubtful hesitation<br>And clarify the conduct on the path to enlightenment! {292} ^F251b

“ ‘As I am swept away by the river, please show me the shore;<br>Be a lamp for me in the darkness.<br>Heal my wounds, O king of physicians,<br>And remove from me the thorn of pain! {293}

“ ‘Liberate me from lower destinies,<br>And sever the clinging of my preoccupation with the world!<br>Deliver me to the other side of the vast river of suffering<br>With the great eightfold path. {294}

“ ‘Life is perishable, our time is short,<br>And there are many obstacles to virtue.<br>My merit will ripen before long.<br>Now that I have attained this opportunity, teach me the only certain thing. {295}

“ ‘Clarify for me, O protector of the world, the following:<br>How should a bodhisattva be conscientious?<br>How does a bodhisattva engage in the supreme enlightened conduct?<br>How shall I liberate the world from the chains of existence?’ {296}

“Thereafter, Rāṣṭrapāla, the Tathāgata Siddhārthabuddhi, upon realizing young prince Puṇyaraśmi’s extraordinary intent, extensively explained to him the conduct of a bodhisattva.

“Listening to him, young prince Puṇyaraśmi attained the dhāraṇī called complete liberation. He also attained the five kinds of superknowledge; and, flying through the air, he emanated flowers and tossed them copiously upon the tathāgata. Next, Rāṣṭrapāla, young prince Puṇyaraśmi descended from the sky and greatly praised the Bhagavān, the Tathāgata Siddhārthabuddhi, with the following verses:

“ ‘I praise you, one who is endowed with the supreme buddha marks, [n93]<br>Undefiled moon-faced being of golden color!<br>I praise you, one who is immaculate, unrivaled in the three worlds,<br>You with unequaled great wisdom! {297}

“ ‘Your hair, O Victorious One, is gentle, beautiful, glossy, and superb.<br>Your uṣṇīṣa is like the king of mountains in this world.<br>One with such a protuberance has never been seen; you are unequaled.<br>Between your eyebrows, O Sage, you have a beautiful hair tuft. {298}

“ ‘It is white like jasmine, the moon, and a conch shell.<br>Your excellent eyes are like blue lotuses—<br>Through them you behold this world with compassion.<br>I praise you, the Victorious One whose eyes are stainless. {299} ^F252a

“ ‘The color of your large and thin tongue is like that of copper.<br>Your tongue can even cover your face.<br>Teaching the Dharma, you tame the world.<br>I praise you, one who has a gentle and tender voice. {300}

“ ‘Your excellent teeth are extremely firm like diamonds.<br>They are forty, straight without gaps.<br>When you smile, you tame the world.<br>I praise you, one whose speech is true and pleasant. {301}

“ ‘Your physique, Victorious One, is unequaled.<br>With your light, you illuminate hundreds of buddhafields.<br>Brahmā and Indra, the protectors of the world,<br>Are outshone by your splendor, Bhagavān. {302}

“ ‘Your antelope-like calves [n94] are unequaled, O Bhagavān!<br>Your gait is like that of the king of elephants, the peacock, and the lion.<br>Bhagavān, you stride gazing a plough’s length ahead,<br>Making the earth and mountain slopes [n95] tremble. {303}

“ ‘Bhagavān, your body is adorned with the buddha marks;<br>Your skin is soft and golden hued.<br>Beings never tire of beholding such a body.<br>Your body is incomparable. {304}

“ ‘I praise you, one who practiced austerities for hundreds of former eons,<br>Who has delighted in all forms of renouncing, taming, and giving,<br>The one endowed with a mind of compassion and loving kindness toward all sentient beings,<br>The possessor of supreme compassion. {305}

“ ‘I praise you, one who is always devoted to generosity and discipline,<br>Who is firmly devoted to patience and diligence,<br>Holder of the luminous splendor of meditative absorption and insight,<br>And holder of unequaled wisdom. {306}

“ ‘You, hero of speech, [n96] conquer bad teachers.<br>You roar like a lion in the assembly.<br>O king of physicians who dispels the three stains,<br>I praise you, one who brings supreme happiness! {307}

“ ‘Your body, speech, and mind, O Sage, are completely pure,<br>Untainted in the three worlds, like a lotus in water.<br>Your Brahmā voice is like the voice of a kalaviṅka bird.<br>I praise you, one who has passed beyond the three worlds. {308} ^F252b

“ ‘You know this world to be like an illusion,<br>Like an actor’s theater, and like a dream;<br>That there is no self, no sentient being, and no life, along with no realms;<br>And that phenomena are mirages, like the moon in water. {309}

“ ‘Beings wander, not knowing that things<br>Are empty, peaceful, and non-arisen.<br>Out of compassion, you cause them to enter the path [n97]<br>With hundreds of skillful means, methods, and principles. {310}

“ ‘You always behold beings<br>Terrified by hundreds of maladies, such as desire.<br>Like a peerless physician, O Sugata, you make the rounds,<br>Completely liberating hundreds of beings. {311}

“ ‘Seeing the world oppressed by birth, old age, death, and pain,<br>Separated from what is dear,<br>Uttering hundreds of lamentations, and always sick,<br>You, O Sage, make the rounds, completely liberating them, out of compassion. {312}

“ ‘The whole world courses through the realms of animals, hungry ghosts, and hell beings,<br>Like a chariot wheel.<br>To simpletons without a leader or protector,<br>You teach the excellent path. {313}

“ ‘The noble path you have taught, unrivaled chief,<br>Was proclaimed by the victorious ones<br>Who were Dharma Lords and who acted for the welfare<br>Of the world in former times. {314}

“ ‘You utter speech that is gentle, tender, and highly pleasing,<br>That causes supreme pleasure, surpassing that of Brahmā,<br>And that outperforms the songs<br>Of gandharvas, kinnaras, and the highest apsarases. {315}

“ ‘Having listened to this teaching,<br>Which is true, honest, completely purified<br>By inexhaustible skillful means, and endowed with limitless qualities,<br>Myriad beings have been led to peace by the three vehicles. {316}

“ ‘By worshiping you, they will obtain<br>The manifold pleasures of gods and men alike. [n98]<br>They will become kings, benefactors to the world<br>And rich and wealthy, with abundant possessions. {317}

“ ‘By developing faith in you, the unequaled,<br>They will become lords of the four continents, supreme cakravartins,<br>Who disclose the ten virtues to the world<br>And obtain the excellent seven precious substances. {318} ^F253a

“ ‘Worshiping you, the Victorious One,<br>They will become Brahmā, Śakra, or lords of the world.<br>They will become lords of gods or lords of Tuṣita,<br>Of the Paranirmita gods, or of the Yāma gods. {319}

“ ‘Worshiping, seeing, and hearing you, the unequaled one<br>Who clears away beings’ many sufferings,<br>Will thus bear fruits:<br>Beings thereby will attain the supreme state beyond aging and death. {320}

“ ‘You, Bhagavān, skilled in and knowledgeable about the path,<br>Cause this world to turn away from lower destinies.<br>You, Bhagavān, establish beings on the noble path—<br>Restful, peaceful, and immaculate. {321}

“ ‘You, treasure of merit, have been striving for merit;<br>Your meritorious activity will never cease.<br>It will not cease for many millions of eons,<br>Until all beings [n99] have attained excellent enlightenment. {322}

“ ‘They will obtain a completely pure buddhafield, [n100]<br>Its radiance like that of the Paranirmita gods—always delighting.<br>In this most excellent buddhafield,<br>Beings are completely pure in terms of body, speech, and mind. {323}

“ ‘Such qualities and many more will be obtained<br>By the persons worshiping the Victorious One,<br>As well as liberation in the higher realms, bliss among humans,<br>And the treasury of merit for all beings. {324}

“ ‘Your fame and renown are widespread<br>In the many hundreds of buddhafields in all directions,<br>Where, Victorious One, the sugatas, among the retinues,<br>Forever extol the litany of your qualities. {325}

“ ‘I praise you, one who frees beings from plague and liberates them,<br>Who is lovely to behold, possessing the highest compassion,<br>Who has pacified the senses, O Bhagavān, rejoicing in peace,<br>Most great, supreme one among men. {326}

“ ‘O Victorious One, upon hearing your teaching,<br>Standing in space, I obtained the five kinds of superknowledge.<br>Upon becoming a hero, equal to a sugata,<br>I will disclose the stainless Dharma to the world! {327} ^F253b

“ ‘May the world attain the state of buddhahood,<br>Through the extensive merit obtained today<br>By praising the Sugata, who has perfected all good qualities<br>And is honored by gods, men, and nāgas.’ {328}

“At this point, Rāṣṭrapāla, the day was dawning, [n101] and King Arciṣmān heard the sound of weeping in the quarters of the young prince’s women. Having heard it, he went with great haste [n102] to the city of Ratipradhāna. Upon arriving there, he asked, ‘Why are you weeping?’ They answered, ‘Because prince Puṇyaraśmi is not here.’ Then, Rāṣṭrapāla, King Arciṣmān, for the sake of the prince, fell to the ground like a felled tree. Upon rising, he wept and searched the city a thousand times. Then, Rāṣṭrapāla, the main deity of that city said this to King Arciṣmān:

“ ‘The prince has departed to the east, great king, in order to behold, praise, worship, and serve the Tathāgata Siddhārthabuddhi.’ Then, Rāṣṭrapāla, to search for him, King Arciṣmān, along with a retinue of ladies of the court and myriad beings, departed for the eastern direction where the prince had gone. He came before the tathāgata, the arhat, the perfect and complete Buddha Siddhārthabuddhi, and, bowing his head toward the feet of that Bhagavān, stood to one side. Standing to one side, King Arciṣmān praised the Bhagavān with the following verses:

“ ‘No one in the three worlds is your equal, let alone a superior.<br>Excellent being, honored by the lord of the gods and the king of the asuras,<br>Beings never tire of gazing at your body;<br>I pay homage to you, glorious ocean of qualities and wisdom. {329}

“ ‘Victorious One, your thirty-two buddha marks are extremely pure.<br>You are like Mount Meru adorned with sublime jewels, completely pure.<br>Victorious One, beautiful, smooth, resembling the color of gold,<br>Pleasant to behold is your physique—to the body of the Sage I pay homage! {330} ^F254a

“ ‘For inconceivable myriad eons, you have practiced firm observance.<br>For inconceivable myriad eons, you have paid respect to the buddhas.<br>You made inconceivable hundreds of offerings in the past.<br>Therefore, your body is beautiful and very captivating. {331}

“ ‘By your generosity, discipline, concentration, and insight,<br>By your patience, diligence, absorption, and skillful means, you have refined your form.<br>In your presence, Indra and Brahmā fade.<br>Even the flash of the moonstones and sunstones [n103] is not as resplendent. {332}

“ ‘For the sake of beings, you manifest a lovely shape.<br>You are present like the moon in the water and an illusion.<br>The Victorious One’s body is present in all our directions.<br>The Sugata’s body is beyond measure. {333}

“ ‘At one time, you resided among the Tuṣita gods.<br>Upon transmigrating and becoming a splendidly white elephant,<br>You, O hero, entered your mother’s womb.<br>Great sage, you extend everywhere, just like the sky. {334}

“ ‘At one time, O Bhagavān, you appeared to take birth in a particular place.<br>You walked seven steps in each of the cardinal directions and announced,<br>“I am the highest lord in the world, together with its gods and men;<br>I will rescue beings from the ocean of suffering.” {335}

“ ‘Sage, although you had no doubts regarding the Dharma,<br>At one time you lived in the world, as if you needed to learn the science of letters.<br>Although you found peace within the sphere of meditative absorption and concentration,<br>You remained for a certain time amidst women. {336}

“ ‘You left father, mother, son, and wives;<br>Your relatives were overcome with suffering, fainting, and weeping.<br>O excellent being, surrounded by a billion gods,<br>Having gone forth, you stayed in the forest on your own. {337}

“ ‘Although you have defeated the four māras a long time ago,<br>You showed yourself in this realm to subdue the māras here as well.<br>Although you turned the inconceivable wheel in earlier times,<br>You showed yourself out of compassion to turn the wheel here as well. {338}

“ ‘Beholding beings, who constantly entertain eternalist views,<br>You say to your retinue, “I will go beyond suffering.”<br>Beholding beings who always delight in saṃsāra,<br>You speak of extinguishment [n104]—peaceful and cooling. {339} ^F254b

“ ‘You are unequaled in merit, wisdom, insight, and skillful means.<br>Your body’s splendor, O Sage, radiates throughout myriad buddhafields.<br>Leaders in all directions offer praise to you.<br>I praise you, O King of Sages, whose experiential sphere reaches everything. {340}

“ ‘I praise you, one who has fully realized the nature of reality.<br>While you are present in the affairs of all sentient beings like an illusion,<br>You actually come and go nowhere, O Sage.<br>I praise you, one who is firmly established, your apparitions being illusory. {341}

“ ‘You, O hero of men, excellently teach the supreme path<br>By which one attains supreme enlightenment for the sake of beings.<br>Having quickly awakened to the nature of reality,<br>I shall teach it for the sake of beings, O hero of men. {342}

“ ‘O hero of men, omniscient Sage, free from disease<br>And unequaled in the three worlds—who could be more extraordinary?<br>May the world attain unexcelled, peaceful, and excellent enlightenment<br>By the excellent merit I accumulated in this praise!’ {343}

“At this point, Rāṣṭrapāla, the Tathāgata Siddhārthabuddhi, upon realizing the extraordinary intent of King Arciṣmān, taught the Dharma in such a way that no one would turn away from unsurpassable, completely perfect enlightenment. Then, Rāṣṭrapāla, young prince Puṇyaraśmi requested the bhagavān, the Tathāgata Siddhārthabuddhi, ‘I beseech you, Bhagavān, to take your meal in our city tomorrow.’ The Bhagavān, out of kindheartedness for young prince Puṇyaraśmi, agreed without uttering a word. Then, Rāṣṭrapāla, young prince Puṇyaraśmi proclaimed to his parents, friends, and women, ‘All of you, rejoice!

“ ‘Would you agree to make the richly ornamented city of Ratipradhāna into a suitable offering for the Tathāgata, without attachment?’—They rejoiced unanimously.  Then, Rāṣṭrapāla, young prince Puṇyaraśmi proceeded to offer the richly ornamented city of Ratipradhāna to the Tathāgata, without attachment. He offered to the Tathāgata, together with his assembly of monks, food with the full range of five hundred flavors. To all these monks he offered a monastery covered in seven precious substances, bedecked with jeweled pathways, and adorned on top with a canopy of jeweled nets. To the right and left, groves of flower trees were carefully planted, beautified with ponds of white lotus flowers. [n105] Beds were swathed in a hundred thousand pieces of calico fabric, stainless on both sides. To each monk he gave flawless robes, and day after day [n106] he offered additional robes. For thirty million years he was not overcome by drowsiness or torpor, and he did not act out of self-cherishing. [n107] Worshiping the buddha, he did not turn his mind to anything else. ^F255a

“For the duration of that period, the concept of desire, the concept of harming others, and the concept of causing harm did not arise. A craving for kingship did not arise. He had no concern for his body or his life force, let alone for anything else. During that time, he completely understood what the Bhagavān had taught, and thus he did not ask the Tathāgata a second time. For the duration of that period, he did not bathe or anoint his body with ghee or sesame oil [n108] or wash his feet, nor did he conceive of his hardships. He never sat down except to eat, defecate, or urinate. When the Tathāgata entered parinirvāṇa, Puṇyaraśmi [n109] had a funeral pyre of red sandalwood erected for his ritual cremation.

“At the very spot where the Tathāgata had been cremated, Puṇyaraśmi [n110] worshiped his relics with all the flowers, garlands, incense, and sounds of musical instruments of the entire world for a hundred thousand years. Thus, having brought together all causes and conditions, he caused as many as eight hundred forty million stūpas to be built.  These stūpas were covered in precious lattices made of the seven precious substances and were sheltered by canopies made of pearl lattices. Moreover, on each stūpa he raised [n111] five hundred parasols made of the seven precious substances; and furthermore, at each stūpa he sounded hundreds of thousands of cymbals. He planted flower trees in all corners of the world. At each stūpa, he lit lamps on a hundred thousand lamp stands. Moreover, on each of these lamp stands, sixty-four thousand lamps with scented oil were lit. Moreover, he worshiped with many varieties of incense, garlands, and ointments. In such ways he worshiped for as many as ten million years, and subsequently became a renunciant. ^F255b

“Having become a renunciant, he put on the three Dharma robes, continually engaged in begging for alms, and always slept in a seated posture without ever lying down. He never became overwhelmed by drowsiness and torpor. With a mind free from worldly concerns, he offered the gift of Dharma for as long as forty million years. He did not expect even appreciation from others, let alone gain and honor. He also never tired of listening to the Dharma or explaining it. Even the gods served him. All people in the country, his retinue of ladies of the court, all his family members, and all his companions followed his example and became renunciants. At this point, Rāṣṭrapāla, the gods of the Śuddhāvāsa realm thought, ‘People of all countries have become renunciants following Puṇyaraśmi’s example. Therefore, if we honor and serve them, we will honor the Three Jewels.’

“Following the parinirvāṇa of the tathāgata, the excellent Dharma had remained for six hundred forty million years, and it was preserved in its entirety by the monk Puṇyaraśmi. In such a way, he worshiped myriad buddhas.

“Now, [n112] Rāṣṭrapāla, in case you hesitate, harbor any doubts, or  have a second thought, wondering whether at that time King Arciṣmān was someone else, you should not do so. Why not? Because at that time, the Tathāgata Amitāyus was indeed King Arciṣmān. Now, Rāṣṭrapāla, in case you hesitate, harbor any doubts, or have a second thought, wondering whether at that time young prince Puṇyaraśmi was someone else, you should not do so. Why not? Because at that time, I was young prince Puṇyaraśmi, and the main deity of the city was the Tathāgata Akṣobhya. Therefore, Rāṣṭrapāla, a bodhisattva mahāsattva who desires to be perfectly awakened to unsurpassable, completely perfect enlightenment should follow the example of young prince Puṇyaraśmi by accomplishing the highest intent, by abandoning pleasant and unpleasant things, and by conscientious conduct. ^F256a

“Through the accomplishment of such difficult tasks, I attained unsurpassable, completely perfect enlightenment. Yet there are those without diligence; those who strive after gain, honor, and glory; those who are attached to public reputation; [n113] those who are overpowered by pride, afflicted, distressed, and far from the teachings due to their desires; those who became renunciants for no good reason; those who are weary of asceticism; those who are unruly bodhisattvas; those who are dishonest in body, speech, and mind; those who are fortune tellers; those who offer false promises; those who do not keep their word; those who are attached to the trappings of clothing, food, bedding, medicine for the sick, and personal belongings; those who have no sense of shame or modesty; those who display bad behavior; those who are devoted to something other than the genuine Dharma; those who are without the experiential sphere of spiritual practice; [n114] those who are far from the experiential sphere of the buddhas; those who are outside the vehicle of the Buddha; and those who do not possess the aspiration to perfect enlightenment. Having heard these teachings, Rāṣṭrapāla, you should know that such people are nefarious friends, unreasonable, motivated only by gain, and not to be associated with.”

Then, the Bhagavān uttered the following verses: ^F256b

“While engaged in the unfathomable practice of acquiring the ten strengths,<br>Their hearts revolve around gain and public reputation.<br>Having abandoned enlightenment, which is replete with a hundred qualities,<br>They delight in frequenting others’ households for the sake of gain. {344}

“Like malicious crows, they are shameless and without devotion.<br>For the sake of public reputation, [n115] they have fallen under the power of Namuci. [n116]<br>Dependent on their afflictions, they drown in the realms of existence<br>And speak thus, ‘Here, we are endowed with good qualities!’ {345}

“Their bodies are in the forest, but their minds dwell on the city. [n117]<br>They practice, but all the while their thoughts are set on procurements.<br>They are as far away from liberation as the sky is from the earth. {346}<br>Drive such people far away like snakes!

“They delight in neither the Buddha, nor the Dharma,<br>Nor the Saṅgha, which is replete with a hundred qualities.<br>Abandoning the higher realms, they go down bad paths.<br>Bereft of happiness, they roam around in the realms of saṃsāra. {347}

“Upon hearing this teaching about my practice,<br>Apply it now by accomplishing the perfect, lofty intent.<br>Seizing this opportunity, so hard to find during myriad eons,<br>You should apply yourself to the nature of reality, just as I have taught it here. {348}

“Those who wish to awaken to the excellent enlightenment of the supreme vehicle<br>Should recall its qualities, O king!<br>Reflecting thoroughly, they should abide in a proper way, according to how things are.<br>The enlightenment of the sugatas is thereby accomplished without hindrance. {349}

“Having realized these qualities, you should attend to noble families.<br>You should indeed generate wisdom here and now. [n118]<br>Don’t abandon such a teaching, which has the essence of qualities,<br>Or you will roam around in the five realms of existence like childish beings! {350}

“Live in mountains, forests, and caves, here and now!<br>Dwelling there, you will neither exalt yourself nor despise others.<br>Continually apply yourself at all times,<br>Remembering that in former times, you did not please the myriad buddhas. {351}

“Abandon without attachment the craving for body and life!<br>Generate deep respect and exert yourself in the Dharma!<br>For those who abide by the practices I explained in this sūtra,<br>It will not be difficult to attain enlightenment. {352}

“Those who practice will delight in the Victorious One’s vehicle.<br>Those who do not practice will turn their minds away after having heard it. [n119]<br>Therefore, you should generate devotion toward the doctrine,<br>Lest you are filled with regret when you deviate in the future. {353}

“Rāṣṭrapāla, suppose one bodhisattva engages in the five perfections, [n120] and the other diligently practices this Dharma discourse, thinking ‘I shall study this. I shall abide by these vows.’  In that case, the amount of the first one’s merit would not come close to even a hundredth part of the latter’s. It would not reach a thousandth, a hundred-thousandth, or a trillionth—it would not even be amenable to any counting, division, calculation, or comparison.” [n121] ^F257a

As the Bhagavān explained this Dharma discourse, all the accompanying thirty billion gods, humans, and asuras who had not previously given rise to the aspiration to enlightenment now gave rise to the mind intent on unsurpassable, perfectly complete enlightenment, without straying away from it. The minds of the seven thousand monks, no longer grasping, were completely liberated from contaminants.

Then the venerable Rāṣṭrapāla asked this of the Bhagavān:

“Bhagavān, what is the name of this Dharma discourse? How should I remember it?” Having thus been asked, the Bhagavān replied to the venerable Rāṣṭrapāla, “In this regard, Rāṣṭrapāla, you should remember this Dharma discourse as The Pure, Meaningful Promise. You should remember it as The Sport of Noble Men and The Ascertainment of the Conduct of a Bodhisattva. And you should also remember it as The Perfect Fulfillment of Meaning.” After the Bhagavān had spoken these words, the venerable Rāṣṭrapāla, as well as the world with its gods, humans, asuras, and gandharvas, rejoiced and praised what the Bhagavān had said.
