# The Verses on Circumambulating Shrines

> **84000 English, stored verbatim as a reference layer** (CC BY-NC-ND — do not excerpt or adapt for output; the shipped verse is our own translation, citing 84000). Tibetan source: `1-SOURCES/Text/bo-toh321.md`. Folio anchors `^F<folio>` align to the Degé Kangyur.

Homage to the Three Jewels. ^F198b

Śāriputra, the greatly wise one,<br>Foremost among those with wisdom, [n1]<br>Who turns the wheel of Dharma in the world,<br>Asked the Teacher a question.

“What karmic result will there be<br>For those who circumambulate a shrine?<br>Illustrious one, supreme in the world,<br>Perfect guide, please tell me.”

The best of those who walk on two feet,<br>The teacher, the perfect Buddha, answered,<br>“I will only teach a portion of the virtues<br>Of those who circumambulate a shrine.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Shall be honored everywhere<br>By devas, nāgas, and likewise by yakṣas,<br>And, indeed, by piśācas and rākṣasas. [n2]

“Those who circumambulate a shrine,<br>Even only a single time,<br>Having gained the opportunity so hard to find,<br>Will be freed from the eight inopportune states. ^F199a

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will possess mindfulness, intelligence,<br>Beauty, and clearsightedness,<br>And be honored everywhere.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will acquire a long lifespan<br>As a god or likewise as a human, [n3]<br>And great renown too.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will be born possessing intelligence [n4]<br>And also a lineage that is pure<br>In the foremost clan in Jambūdvīpa.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will have a kind and pure countenance,<br>A good complexion, and be bright too. [n5]<br>They will dwell in happiness.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will have great wealth and riches.<br>They will not be miserly, but generous,<br>And will take pleasure in giving.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will achieve great prosperity.<br>They will be beautiful and gorgeous,<br>With an attractive and lovely skin tone.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will not be deluded about phenomena,<br>Seeing conditioned things as empty, [n6]<br>And will quickly obtain happiness.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will be born as a most excellent kṣatriya,<br>Surrounded by a wife and children,<br>And endowed with strength and vigor.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will be born as a great landowning brahmin,<br>Endowed with moral discipline and learning,<br>With knowledge of the Vedas and mantras.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will be born as a great landowning householder,<br>Having much wealth and many riches,<br>With grain and cattle in abundance.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will become lord and sovereign of Jambūdvīpa,<br>A righteous king whose rule extends<br>To the ends of the earth.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will become a wheel-turning king,<br>Possessing the seven royal treasures,<br>And roll the wheel according to the Dharma. ^F199b

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will go to higher realms once they pass on from here,<br>Find joy in the Buddha’s teachings, [n7]<br>Become yogins, and gain great miraculous powers.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine,<br>After passing on from the divine realms,<br>Will be reborn again among humans,<br>Entering the womb without delusion.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will not become tainted<br>By the impurities and flaws of the womb;<br>They will be like a pure jewel. [n8]

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will dwell at ease inside their mothers.<br>They will be at ease as they are born;<br>At ease they will suckle their mothers’ breasts.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will be supported and nourished by many servants,<br>As well as by their fathers,<br>And always cared for by nurses. [n9]

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will prosper right from birth,<br>Be dearly beloved of relatives,<br>And especially doted upon by parents.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will not be able to be harmed [n10]<br>By piśācas or rākṣasas.<br>They will enjoy unhindered abundance.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will have an immaculate body<br>And never be blind or crippled,<br>Through hundreds of eons.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will have perfectly clear eyes,<br>Long and shapely, dark, alluring,<br>And also obtain the divine eye.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will be steady in body, [n11] steady in mind,<br>Steady too in diligence,<br>And have steady, rounded shoulders. [n12]

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will be strong, perfect in shape and form,<br>And their limbs will be well adorned<br>With all auspicious marks.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will be the great lord of the gods,<br>Śakra, whose miraculous powers are great,<br>Lord of the Heaven of the Thirty-Three. ^F200a

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will be king of the gods in the Heaven Without Strife,<br>Or likewise in the Heavens of Joy,<br>Joyful Emanation, or Controlling Others’ Emanations. [n13]

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will be lord of brahmās<br>In the Brahmā World,<br>Honored by tens of millions of gods.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will have wisdom and continually be honored<br>Over tens of billions of eons or, indeed,<br>For millions upon billions of eons.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will have clean bodies, clean clothing,<br>And the immaculate Dharma,<br>For tens of millions of eons.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will be endowed with strength and vigor,<br>Have no laziness at all,<br>And achieve the greatest accomplishment.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will be steady in diligence, steady in power,<br>Steady in skill, be their own master,<br>And swiftly accomplish their goals.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will have melodious speech,<br>A fine, pleasant voice,<br>And be free from harm and illness.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will quickly achieve a rebirth<br>In a place I myself have described<br>And have a great urgency there. [n14]

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will obtain the four applications of mindfulness,<br>The four immeasurable states of mind,<br>And control over the bases of miraculous power.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will obtain the four noble truths,<br>The faculties, the powers,<br>And the fruition of the factors of awakening.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will become arhats, ones with great miraculous powers,<br>Possessing the six supernormal faculties,<br>Without defilement, and all their afflictions dispelled.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will release attachment and aversion,<br>Release all enmities, [n15]<br>And achieve the awakening of a pratyekabuddha.

“Those who circumambulate a shrine<br>Will become a tathāgata in the world,<br>With a body the color of gold<br>And adorned with the marks. ^F200b

“Circumambulation is an act of the body.<br>Circumambulation is an act of speech.<br>Circumambulation is an act of the mind.<br>Circumambulation is also the making of a vow.<br>Here, one easily achieves through circumambulation<br>Goals that are difficult to reach. [n16]

“Mere words cannot well convey,<br>Even slightly, the virtues of those<br>Who perform a circumambulation<br>At a shrine of the World Protector.”

When asked by Śāriputra,<br>The World Protector explained<br>The virtues of venerating shrines,<br>Out of compassion for beings.

“A hundred horses, a hundred gold pieces,<br>A hundred mule carts,<br>And a hundred mare-drawn carriages<br>Filled with all manner of jewels<br>Do not add up to even a sixteenth of the worth<br>Of a single step of a circumambulation. [n17]

“A hundred Kamboja girls [n18]<br>Wearing jeweled earrings and bangles,<br>Their upper arms bearing golden armlets,<br>Their necks well adorned with gold necklaces,<br>Do not add up to even a sixteenth of the worth<br>Of a single step of a circumambulation.

“A hundred Himalayan elephants, [n19]<br>With huge bodies, broad chests,<br>And trunks like plows,<br>Adorned with gold and jewels,<br>Do not add up to even a sixteenth of the worth<br>Of a single step of a circumambulation.

“Hundreds of thousands of gold pieces from the Jambu River<br>Are not equal to a single step<br>Taken with a thought of joy by a wise one [n20]<br>At shrines of a buddha. [n21]

“Hundreds of thousands of nuggets [n22] of Jambu River gold<br>Are not equal to a single lump of clay<br>Offered with a thought of joy by a wise one<br>At shrines of a buddha.

“Hundreds of thousands of bowls of Jambu River gold<br>Are not equal to a handful of flower petals<br>Scattered with a thought of joy by a wise one<br>At shrines of a buddha. ^F201a

“Tens of millions of bushels of Jambu River gold<br>Are not equal to a flower garland<br>Held up with a thought of joy by a wise one<br>At shrines of a buddha.

“Hundreds of thousands of heaps of Jambu River gold<br>Are not equal to scented water<br>Sprinkled with a thought of joy by a wise one<br>At shrines of a buddha.

“Hundreds of thousands of grams of Jambu River gold<br>Are not equal to an oil lamp<br>Lit with a thought of joy by a wise one<br>At shrines of a buddha.

“Hundreds of thousands of mountains of gold<br>Are not equal to a banner or canopy<br>Raised with a thought of joy by a wise one<br>At shrines of a buddha.

“This is the teaching on the benefits<br>Of making offerings to tathāgatas,<br>Perfect buddhas, peerless caravan leaders,<br>Who are limitless, just like the ocean. [n23]

“Whether one makes offerings to a living buddha<br>Or makes them to one who has passed into nirvāṇa,<br>There is no difference in merit<br>If the thought is equally devout. [n24]

“In this way, buddhas are inconceivable;<br>A buddha’s qualities are also inconceivable.<br>For those who have faith [n25] in the inconceivable,<br>The karmic fruit is also inconceivable.” [n26]


## Notes (84000)

[n1] Following the Cambridge Sanskrit MS, which reads yo ’graḥ prajñāvatāṃ, and a handful of Kangyurs, such as London and Namgyal, which have shes rab ldan mchog. Most editions of the Kangyur, including Degé, Stok, and those collated in the Pedurma, read shes rab blo ldan, “possessing wisdom and intelligence.”

[n2] Following Stok, which reads: lha klu de bzhin gnod sbyin dang// sha za de bzhin srin rnams kyis// thams cad du yang mchod par ’gyur. Stok is supported by the Cambridge MS, which reads devair nāgais tathā yakṣaiḥ | piśācair atha rākṣasaiḥ | sarvatra pūjāṃ prāpnoti…. The last three lines of the Degé read a bit differently here, lha klu gnod sbyin dri za dang // lha min mkha’ lding mi’am ci// lto ’phye che rnams mchod byed ’gyur, “…shall be honored by devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kinnaras, and mahoragas.”

[n3] The Degé reads lha dang de bzhin mi rnams kyis, while the Cambridge MS has divyaṃ mānuṣyakaṃ tathā.

[n4] The Tibetan reads blo ldan, “possessing intelligence,” while the Cambridge MS has vīro, “a hero.”

[n5] Here and in verse 6, the Cambridge MS uses the same word, vicakṣaṇa (“clear sighted” or “bright”), whereas the Degé in verse 6 has mdzangs pa and here reads mkhas pa, “wise.” We have chosen “bright” here because it conveys both the physical and mental quality of the term.

[n6] The Tibetan reads ’du byed stong par mthong na kyang, apparently reading dṛṣṭvā saṃskāraśunyatāṃ, whereas the Cambridge MS reads dṛṣṭvā saṃsāraśunyatāṃ, “seeing saṃsāra as empty.”

[n7] The Tibetan has sang rgyas bstan la dga’ ba dang, while the Cambridge MS reads prasanno buddhaśāsane. This is the first of several instances in this Tibetan translation in which the Tibetan term dga’ ba, more commonly used to translate words meaning “joy,” appears to translate the Sanskrit term prasanna, which typically means “faithful,” “serenely confident,” or “trusting,” but which can also sometimes mean “pleased” or “glad.” Other occurrences of these terms are found in verses 49–55 and 57, while in the translation of verse 58 the Tibetan translates prasanna with dad, “faithful.”

[n8] The Tibetan has de ni nor bu dag pa bzhin, while the Cambridge MS reads maṇivatpariśuddho sau, “…pure like a jewel.”

[n9] Tib. ma ma and Skt. dhātrī may indicate female caregivers more broadly, such as wet nurses, nannies, and so on, and may even refer to mothers, though the former sense seems the more primary usage. The overall sense of the verse suggests something about the affluent circumstances into which one is born.

[n10] For this line, S reads de la gnod ba mi nus te, while D has de la gnod par mi ’gyur te, but the Cambridge MS, which has nāsau laṃghayituṃśakyaḥ, would seem to support the former.

[n11] S has lus brtan, while D reads lus bde, “comfortable body,” but the Cambridge MS has sthirakāya, which again supports the former.

[n12] Tib. dpung mgo zlum zhing brtan par ’gyur. “Rounded shoulders” (Tib. dpung mgo zlum, Skt. saṃvṛttaskandha) are among the thirty-two auspicious marks of a great being. This line may indicate a variation, since the Cambridge MS reads sthirasthaḥ sarvadharmeṣu, “abides in steadiness in regard to all things.”

[n13] The Degé has ’thab bral lha yi rgyal po, “king of the gods of the Heaven Without Strife,” while S has ’thab bral lha yi dbang po, “lords of the gods of the Heaven Without Strife,” and the Cambridge MS has suyāmo devaputraś, “the god Suyāma,” who is said to be the divine ruler of the Yāma Heaven or Heaven Without Strife.

[n14] Tib. shas cher, Skt. samvegabahula. The concept of saṃvega (“agitation,” “intensity”) is one that is sometimes applied in Buddhist literature to persons who are practicing the Buddhist path with a persistent mindset.

[n15] The Degé has’khor rnams tham cad, “all assemblies,” but the Stok has khon rnams tham cad…, which is indeed supported by the Cambridge MS, which reads sarvavaira.

[n16] In Tibetan, this verse interrupts the preceding series of four-line verses with a verse of six lines. This six-line verse appears to be an elaboration of the extant Sanskrit versions from Gilgit and Central Asia, on which see Melzer 2010, p. 64. However, it is supported textually, at least in part, by the Cambridge MS, which includes the line praṇidhiśca pradakṣiṇaṃ (Tib. smon lam btab pa’ang bskor ba yin), “circumambulation is also the taking of a vow,” which none of the others have. Nonetheless, the Cambridge MS still does not seem to contain anything corresponding to the Tibetan bgrod dka’ bde dgur…, “easily” and “…that are difficult to reach.” Further work is required here to establish a textual basis for the underlying Sanskrit.

[n17] In this and the following two verses, the Tibetan has bskor ba’i gom pa gcig, “a single step of a circumambulation,” but none of the extant Sanskrit versions, including the Cambridge MS, include a word corresponding to bskor ba, in keeping with what seems to be the original context of their utterance in the Vinaya. It is worth noting that the Tibetan translation of the Vinaya passage does not include the word bskor ba either. In the Tibetan, verses 46–48 are six-line verses with seven syllables per line.

[n18] D kam bod za yi bu mo brgya; Skt śataṃ kāmbojikākānyāḥ; S tho gar yul gyi bu mo brgya (“one hundred Tocharian girls”).

[n19] The Tibetan has gangs kyi glang chen, “elephants of the snows,” while the Sanskrit has haimavatā nāgāḥ, haimavat being another word for himālaya, which in Sanskrit means “abode of snow.”

[n20] Here and in the next several verses, Tib. mkhas pa gang gis, “by a wise one,” and dga’ ba’i sems kyis, “with a thought of joy,” translate Skt. vidvān and prasannacittaḥ, respectively. For dga’ ba’i sems / prasannacitta, we have chosen to prioritize the Tibetan interpretation, although other renderings are possible. For more on these terms, see the introduction and notes and for further discussion.

[n21] In the Tibetan, verses 49–55 are four-line verses with nine syllables per line, reflecting the fact that the meter of the Sanskrit verses also changes from eight syllables to eleven syllables per line.

[n22] The Degé has pho drang, “palaces,” while the Stok has pha bong, “nuggets,” which works better with the comparison made in the verse and is closer to the Sanskrit piṇḍā.

[n23] The final three verses in the Tibetan translation return to a pattern of four lines of seven syllables each, reflecting a return to anuṣṭubh meter in the Sanskrit.

[n24] Here we have chosen to vary the translation of dga’ ba in the phrase sems dga’ ba ni mtshungs ’gyur na, which translates saṃaṃ cittaṃ prasādyeha, choosing “devout” as a translation for it here even though “joyful” would remain consistent with the Tibetan interpretation. The term again translates a form of the Sanskrit verb prasad, “to settle down,” “to become clear,” “to be pleased or glad,” “to have faith or trust,” of which prasanna is the past passive participle. For more on this verse and the general idea it expresses, see Schopen 1997, pp. 131–33 and note 75.

[n25] Here the Tibetan translation of prasanna varies from dga’ ba to dad rnams, “those who have faith.”

[n26] This verse has parallels or close variants in other canonical texts, including the Vinaya passages cited in the introduction, as well as Toh 189 () and Toh 846a ().
