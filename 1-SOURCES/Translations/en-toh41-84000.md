# The Sūtra of the Sun

> **84000 English, stored verbatim as a reference layer** (CC BY-NC-ND — do not excerpt or adapt for output; the shipped verse is our own translation, citing 84000). Tibetan source: `1-SOURCES/Text/bo-toh41.md`. Folio anchors `^F<folio>` align to the Degé Kangyur.

Respectful homage to the noble Three Jewels! ^F282a

Thus did I hear at one time. The Blessed One was dwelling in Śrāvastī, in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍada’s Park. [n1] At that time the god Sūrya was seized by Rāhu, lord of the asuras. Then  the god Sūrya, recollecting and taking the Blessed One to heart, [n2] recited this verse: ^F282b

“I bow to the heroic Buddha. [n3]<br>May you swiftly liberate all beings! [n4]<br>As I have come into confinement<br>I take refuge in you.” [n5]

Then the Blessed One, for the sake of the god Sūrya, addressed Rāhu, lord of the asuras, with these verses:

“Since buddhas have compassion for the world,<br>Sūrya took refuge<br>In the Worthy One, the Tathāgata.<br>Therefore, Rāhu, set the sun free! [n6]

“The sun, circle of mighty splendor,<br>Illuminates darkness with light.<br>Rāhu, do not conceal the sun in the sky!<br>Set this sun free, Rāhu!” [n7]

Thereupon Rāhu, lord of the asuras, set the god Sūrya free and hurriedly [n8] approached Vemacitra, [n9] lord of the asuras. He then sat to one side, displeased, [n10] agitated, and with all his body hairs bristling. Vemacitra, lord of the asuras, now questioned Rāhu, lord of the asuras, in verse:

“Why, Rāhu, did you release<br>The sun in such a fright?<br>You are a very miserable sight.<br>Why did you approach in fear?” [n11]

He replied: [n12]

“Had I, hearing the Buddha’s verse,<br>Not then released the sun<br>My head would have split into seven pieces [n13]<br>And my life would be without happiness.”


## Notes (84000)

[n1] The Paritta collection gives the setting in full, as does the Tibetan, but it is abbreviated as sāvatthinidānaṃ in the SN.

[n2] “Taking to heart” is missing in the Pali. Alternatively, it is possible that the Tibetan phrase bcom ldan ’das rjes su dran pa yid la byas is an attempt to translate the Pali bhagavantam anussaramāno and might be rendered “bringing to mind the remembrance of the Blessed One.”

[n3] The Pali reads namo te buddha vīratthu (“Homage to you, Buddha, heroic one!”).

[n4] Here the Pali reads vippamuttosi sabbadhi (“You are completely liberated!”).

[n5] The Pali reads sambādhapaṭipannosmi, tassa me saraṇaṃ bhavā”ti (“I have entered confinement, be my refuge!”). Though the Tibetan translation bdag ’di’i kha ru bcug pas na/ /bdag ni khyed la skyabs su mchi, the first part of which seems to say, “As I have entered into the mouth of it,” seems distant at first, upon reflection it conveys the meaning of the Pali well.

[n6] The causal connections in this verse are not explicit in the Pali.

[n7] The Pali reads yo andhakāre tamasi pabhaṅkaro, verocano maṇḍalī uggatejo, mā rāhu gilī caramantalikkhe, pajaṃ mamaṃ rāhu pamuñca sūriyan”ti (“He lights up darkness and gloom, / The sun, circle of mighty splendour. / Rāhu, do not swallow the sun as he traverses the sky! / Rāhu, set my offspring, the sun, free!”). This verse is missing in Toh 42 and in its Pali source, but it has a parallel in Toh 331 and in its Sanskrit and Chinese counterparts.

[n8] Here translated with reference to the Pali taramānarūpo yena vepacitti asurindo tenupasaṅkami. The Tibetan phrase sngar gyi gzugs bzhin du corresponds to the Pali taramānarūpo, which means “hurriedly.”

[n9] In Toh 331 Rāhu’s interlocutor is Bali. These asuras are often associated, and are accredited with the role of leaders. See The White Lotus of the Good Dharma, Toh 113 (1.8) and The Play in Full, Toh 95 (16.15).

[n10] Missing in the Pali.

[n11] The Pali has kiṃ nu santaramānova, rāhu sūriyaṃ pamuñcasi; saṃviggarūpo āgamma, kiṃ nu bhītova tiṭṭhasī”ti (“Why did you, Rāhu, in a hurry, release the sun? After having approached, moved by fear, why are you standing here so scared?”).

[n12] Missing in the Pali.

[n13] This theme is found in parallel sūtras and elsewhere in the Kangyur. See The White Lotus of the Good Dharma, Toh 113 (21.20), The Hundred Deeds, Toh 340 (6.196), The Root Manual of the Rites of Mañjuśrī, Toh 543 (15.107) and Destroyer of the Great Trichiliocosm, Toh 558 (1.286)
