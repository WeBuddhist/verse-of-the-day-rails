# Dhammapada — English (Bhikkhu Sujato, CC0)

## 1. Pairs ^vagga-1-0

Intention shapes experiences;<br>intention is first, they’re made by intention.<br>If with corrupt intent<br>you speak or act,<br>suffering follows you,<br>like a wheel, the ox’s foot. ^1

Intention shapes experiences;<br>intention is first, they’re made by intention.<br>If with pure intent<br>you speak or act,<br>happiness follows you<br>like a shadow that never leaves. ^2

“They abused me, they hit me!<br>They beat me, they robbed me!”<br>For those who bear such a grudge,<br>hatred is never laid to rest. ^3

“They abused me, they hit me!<br>They beat me, they robbed me!”<br>For those who bear no such grudge,<br>hatred is laid to rest. ^4

For never is hatred<br>laid to rest by hate,<br>it’s laid to rest by love:<br>this is an ancient teaching. ^5

When others do not understand,<br>let us, who do understand this,<br>restrain ourselves in this regard;<br>for that is how conflicts are laid to rest. ^6

Those who contemplate the beautiful,<br>their faculties unrestrained,<br>immoderate in eating,<br>lazy, lacking energy:<br>Māra strikes them down<br>like the wind, a feeble tree. ^7

Those who contemplate the ugly,<br>their faculties well-restrained,<br>eating in moderation,<br>faithful and energetic:<br>Māra cannot strike them down,<br>like the wind, a rocky mountain. ^8

One who, not free of stains themselves,<br>would wear the robe stained in ocher,<br>bereft of self-control and of truth:<br>they are not worthy of the ocher robe. ^9

One who’s purged all their stains,<br>steady in ethics,<br>possessed of self-control and of truth,<br>they are truly worthy of the ocher robe. ^10

Thinking the inessential is essential,<br>seeing the essential as inessential;<br>they don’t realize the essential,<br>for wrong thoughts are their habitat. ^11

Having known the essential as essential,<br>and the inessential as inessential;<br>they realize the essential,<br>for right thoughts are their habitat. ^12

Just as rain seeps into<br>a poorly roofed house,<br>lust seeps into<br>an undeveloped mind. ^13

Just as rain doesn’t seep into<br>a well roofed house,<br>lust doesn’t seep into<br>a well developed mind. ^14

Here they grieve, hereafter they grieve,<br>an evildoer grieves in both places.<br>They grieve and fret,<br>seeing their own corrupt deeds. ^15

Here they rejoice, hereafter they rejoice,<br>one who does good rejoices in both places.<br>They rejoice and celebrate,<br>seeing their own pure deeds. ^16

Here they’re tormented, <j>hereafter they’re tormented,<br>an evildoer is tormented in both places.<br>They’re tormented <j>thinking of bad things they’ve done;<br>when gone to a bad place, <j>they’re tormented all the more. ^17

Here they delight, hereafter they delight,<br>one who does good delights in both places.<br>They delight thinking of good things they’ve done;<br>when gone to a good place, they delight all the more. ^18

Much though they may recite scripture,<br>if a negligent person does not apply them,<br>then, like a cowherd who counts the cattle of others,<br>they miss out on the blessings of the ascetic life. ^19

Little though they may recite scripture,<br>if they live in line with the teaching,<br>having given up greed, hate, and delusion,<br>with deep understanding and heart well freed,<br>not grasping to this life or the next,<br>they share in the blessings of the ascetic life. ^20

## 2. Diligence ^vagga-2-0

Heedfulness is the state free of death;<br>heedlessness is the state of death.<br>The heedful do not die,<br>while the heedless are like the dead. ^21

Understanding this distinction<br>when it comes to heedfulness,<br>the astute rejoice in heedfulness,<br>happy in the noble ones’ domain. ^22

They who regularly meditate,<br>always staunchly vigorous;<br>the attentive realize extinguishment,<br>the supreme sanctuary from the yoke. ^23

For the hard-working and mindful,<br>pure of deed and attentive,<br>restrained, living righteously, and diligent,<br>their reputation only grows. ^24

By hard work and diligence,<br>by restraint and by self-control,<br>a smart person would build an island<br>that the floods cannot overflow. ^25

Fools and simpletons<br>devote themselves to negligence.<br>But the wise protect diligence<br>as their best treasure. ^26

Don’t devote yourself to negligence,<br>or delight in erotic intimacy.<br>For if you’re diligent and meditate,<br>you’ll attain abundant happiness. ^27

When the astute dispel negligence<br>by means of diligence,<br>ascending the palace of wisdom,<br>sorrowless, they behold this generation of sorrow,<br>as an attentive one on a mountain top<br>beholds the fools below. ^28

Heedful among the heedless,<br>wide awake while others sleep—<br>a true sage leaves them behind,<br>like a swift horse passing a feeble. ^29

Maghavā became chief of the gods<br>by means of diligence.<br>People praise diligence,<br>while negligence is always deplored. ^30

A mendicant who loves diligence,<br>seeing fear in negligence—<br>advances like fire,<br>burning up fetters big and small. ^31

A mendicant who loves diligence,<br>seeing fear in negligence—<br>such a one can’t decline,<br>and has drawn near to extinguishment. ^32

## 3. The Mind ^vagga-3-0

The mind quivers and shakes,<br>hard to guard, hard to curb.<br>The discerning straighten it out,<br>like a fletcher straightens an arrow. ^33

Like a fish pulled from the sea<br>and cast upon the shore,<br>this mind flounders about,<br>trying to throw off Māra’s dominion. ^34

Hard to hold back, flighty,<br>alighting where it will;<br>it’s good to tame the mind;<br>a tamed mind leads to bliss. ^35

So hard to see, so subtle,<br>alighting where it will;<br>the discerning protect the mind,<br>a guarded mind leads to bliss. ^36

The mind travels far, wandering alone;<br>incorporeal, it lies hidden in the heart.<br>Those who will restrain the mind<br>are freed from Māra’s bonds. ^37

Those of unsteady mind,<br>who don’t understand the true teaching,<br>and whose confidence wavers,<br>do not perfect their wisdom. ^38

One whose mind is not festering,<br>whose heart is undamaged,<br>who’s given up right and wrong,<br>alert, has nothing to fear. ^39

Knowing this body breaks like a pot,<br>and fortifying the mind like a citadel,<br>attack Māra with the sword of wisdom,<br>guard your conquest, and never settle. ^40

All too soon this body<br>will lie upon the earth,<br>bereft of consciousness,<br>tossed aside like a worthless log. ^41

A wrongly directed mind<br>would do you more harm<br>than a hater to the hated,<br>or an enemy to their foe. ^42

A rightly directed mind<br>would do you more good<br>than your mother or father<br>or any other relative. ^43

## 4. Flowers ^vagga-4-0

Who bestirs this earth,<br>and the Yama realm with its gods?<br>Who sets out the well-taught word of truth,<br>as an expert a flower? ^44

A trainee bestirs this earth,<br>and the Yama realm with its gods.<br>A trainee sets out the well-taught word of truth,<br>as an expert a flower. ^45

Knowing this body’s like foam,<br>realizing it’s all just a mirage,<br>and cutting off Māra’s blossoming,<br>vanish from the King of Death. ^46

As a mighty flood sweeps off a sleeping village,<br>death steals away a man<br>even as he gathers flowers,<br>his mind caught up in them. ^47

The terminator gains control of the man<br>who has not had his fill of pleasures,<br>even as he gathers flowers,<br>his mind caught up in them. ^48

A bee takes the nectar<br>and moves on, doing no damage<br>to the flower’s beauty and fragrance;<br>and that’s how a sage should walk in the village. ^49

Don’t find fault with others,<br>with what they’ve done or left undone.<br>You should only watch yourself,<br>what you’ve done or left undone. ^50

Just like a glorious flower<br>that’s colorful but lacks fragrance;<br>eloquent speech is fruitless<br>for one who does not act on it. ^51

Just like a glorious flower<br>that’s both colorful and fragrant,<br>eloquent speech is fruitful<br>for one who acts on it. ^52

Just as one would create many garlands<br>from a heap of flowers,<br>when a person has come to be born,<br>they should do many skillful things. ^53

The fragrance of flowers doesn’t spread upwind,<br>nor sandalwood, pinwheel, or jasmine;<br>but the fragrance of the good spreads upwind;<br>a true person’s virtue spreads in every direction. ^54

Among all the fragrances—<br>sandalwood or pinwheel<br>or lotus or jasmine—<br>the fragrance of virtue is supreme. ^55

Faint is the fragrance<br>of sandal or pinwheel;<br>but the fragrance of the virtuous<br>floats to the highest gods. ^56

For those accomplished in ethics,<br>meditating diligently,<br>freed through the highest knowledge,<br>Māra cannot find their path. ^57

From a heap of trash<br>discarded on the highway,<br>a lotus might blossom,<br>fragrant and delightful. ^58

So too, among those thought of as trash,<br>a disciple of the perfect Buddha<br>outshines with their wisdom<br>the blind ordinary folk. ^59

## 5. The Fool ^vagga-5-0

Long is the night for the wakeful;<br>long is the league for the weary;<br>long transmigrate the fools<br>who don’t understand the true teaching. ^60

If while wandering you find no partner<br>equal or better than yourself,<br>then firmly resolve to wander alone—<br>there’s no fellowship with fools. ^61

“Sons are mine, wealth is mine”—<br>thus the fool frets.<br>For even your self is not your own,<br>let alone your sons or wealth. ^62

The fool who thinks they’re a fool<br>is wise at least to that extent.<br>But the true fool is said to be one<br>who imagines that they are wise. ^63

Though a fool attends to the wise<br>even for the rest of their life,<br>they still don’t understand the teaching,<br>like a spoon the taste of the soup. ^64

If a clever person attends to the wise<br>even just for an hour or so,<br>they swiftly understand the teaching,<br>like a tongue the taste of the soup. ^65

Fools and simpletons behave<br>like their own worst enemies,<br>doing wicked deeds<br>that ripen as bitter fruit. ^66

It’s not good to do a deed<br>that plagues you later on,<br>for which you weep and wail,<br>as its effect stays with you. ^67

It is good to do a deed<br>that doesn’t plague you later on,<br>that gladdens and cheers,<br>as its effect stays with you. ^68

The fool imagines that evil is sweet,<br>so long as it has not yet ripened.<br>But as soon as that evil ripens,<br>they fall into suffering. ^69

Month after month a fool may eat<br>food from a grass-blade’s tip;<br>but they’ll never be worth a sixteenth part<br>of one who has appraised the teaching. ^70

For a wicked deed that has been done<br>does not curdle quickly like milk.<br>Smoldering, it follows the fool,<br>like a fire smothered over with ash. ^71

Whatever fame a fool may get,<br>it only gives rise to harm.<br>Whatever good features they have it ruins,<br>and blows their head into bits. ^72

They’d seek the esteem that they lack,<br>and status among the mendicants;<br>authority over monasteries,<br>and honor among other families. ^73

“Let both layfolk and renunciants think<br>the work was done by me alone.<br>In anything at all that’s to be done,<br>let them fall under my sway alone.”<br>So thinks the fool,<br>their greed and pride only growing. ^74

For the means to profit and the path to quenching<br>are two quite different things.<br>A mendicant disciple of the Buddha,<br>understanding what this really means,<br>would never delight in honors,<br>but rather would foster seclusion. ^75

## 6. The Astute ^vagga-6-0

Regard one who sees your faults<br>as a guide to a hidden treasure.<br>Stay close to one so wise and astute<br>who corrects you when you need it.<br>Sticking close to such an impartial person,<br>things get better, not worse. ^76

Advise and instruct;<br>curb wickedness:<br>for you shall be loved by the good,<br>and disliked by the bad. ^77

Don’t mix with bad friends,<br>nor with the worst of men.<br>Mix with spiritual friends,<br>and with the best of men. ^78

Through joy in the teaching you sleep at ease,<br>with clear and confident heart.<br>An astute person always delights in the teaching<br>proclaimed by the Noble One. ^79

Irrigators guide water,<br>fletchers straighten arrows,<br>carpenters carve timber,<br>the astute tame themselves. ^80

As the wind cannot stir<br>a solid mass of rock,<br>so too blame and praise<br>do not affect the wise. ^81

Like a deep lake,<br>clear and unclouded,<br>so clear are the astute<br>when they hear the teachings. ^82

True persons give up everything,<br>they don’t cajole for the things they desire.<br>Though touched by sadness or happiness,<br>the astute appear neither depressed nor elated. ^83

Never wish for success by unjust means,<br>for your own sake or that of another,<br>desiring children, wealth, or nation;<br>rather, be virtuous, wise, and just. ^84

Few are those among humans<br>who cross to the far shore.<br>The rest just run around<br>on the near shore. ^85

When the teaching is well explained,<br>those who practice accordingly<br>will cross over<br>Death’s dominion so hard to pass. ^86

Rid of dark qualities,<br>an astute person should develop the bright.<br>Leaving home behind<br>for the seclusion so hard to enjoy, ^87

try to find satisfaction there,<br>having left behind sensual pleasures.<br>Owning nothing, an astute person<br>would cleanse themselves of mental corruptions. ^88

Those whose minds are rightly developed<br>in the awakening factors;<br>who, letting go of attachments,<br>delight in not grasping:<br>with defilements ended, brilliant,<br>they are quenched in this world. ^89

## 7. The Perfected Ones ^vagga-7-0

At journey’s end, rid of sorrow;<br>everywhere free,<br>all ties given up,<br>no fever is found in them. ^90

The mindful apply themselves;<br>they delight in no abode.<br>Like a swan gone from the marsh,<br>they leave home after home behind. ^91

Those with nothing stored up,<br>who have understood their food,<br>whose domain is the liberation<br>of the signless and the empty:<br>their path is hard to trace,<br>like birds in the sky. ^92

One whose defilements have ended;<br>who’s not attached to food;<br>whose domain is the liberation<br>of the signless and the empty:<br>their track is hard to trace,<br>like birds in the sky. ^93

Whose faculties have become serene,<br>like horses tamed by a charioteer,<br>who has abandoned conceit and defilements;<br>the unaffected one is envied by even the gods. ^94

Undisturbed like the earth,<br>true to their vows, steady as Indra’s pillar,<br>like a lake clear of mud;<br>such a one does not transmigrate. ^95

Their mind is peaceful,<br>peaceful are their speech and deeds.<br>Such a one is at peace,<br>rightly freed through enlightenment. ^96

Lacking faith, a house-breaker,<br>one who acknowledges nothing,<br>purged of hope, they’ve wasted their chance:<br>that is indeed the supreme person! ^97

Whether in village or wilderness,<br>in a valley or the uplands,<br>wherever the perfected ones live<br>is a delightful place. ^98

Delightful are the wildernesses<br>where no people delight.<br>Those free of greed will delight there,<br>not those who seek sensual pleasures. ^99

## 8. The Thousands ^vagga-8-0

Better than a thousand<br>meaningless sayings<br>is a single meaningful saying,<br>hearing which brings you peace. ^100

Better than a thousand<br>meaningless verses<br>is a single meaningful verse,<br>hearing which brings you peace. ^101

Better than reciting<br>a hundred meaningless verses<br>is a single saying of Dhamma,<br>hearing which brings you peace. ^102

The supreme conqueror is<br>not he who conquers a million men in battle,<br>but he who conquers a single man:<br>himself. ^103

It is surely better to conquer oneself<br>than all those other folk.<br>When a person has tamed themselves,<br>always living restrained, ^104

no god nor centaur,<br>nor Māra nor divinity,<br>can undo the victory<br>of such a personage. ^105

Rather than a thousandfold sacrifice,<br>every month for a full century,<br>it’s better to honor for a single hour<br>one who has developed themselves.<br>That offering is better<br>than the hundred year sacrifice. ^106

Rather than serve the sacred flame<br>in the forest for a hundred years,<br>it’s better to honor for a single hour<br>a personage who has developed themselves.<br>That offering is better<br>than the hundred year sacrifice. ^107

Whatever sacrifice or offering in the world<br>a seeker of merit may make for a year,<br>none of it is worth a quarter<br>of bowing to the sincere. ^108

For one in the habit of bowing,<br>always honoring the elders,<br>four blessings grow:<br>lifespan, beauty, happiness, and strength. ^109

Better to live a single day<br>ethical and absorbed in meditation<br>than to live a hundred years<br>unethical and lacking immersion. ^110

Better to live a single day<br>wise and absorbed in meditation<br>than to live a hundred years<br>witless and lacking immersion. ^111

Better to live a single day<br>energetic and strong,<br>than to live a hundred years<br>lazy and lacking energy. ^112

Better to live a single day<br>seeing rise and fall<br>than to live a hundred years<br>blind to rise and fall. ^113

Better to live a single day<br>seeing the state free of death<br>than to live a hundred years<br>blind to the state free of death. ^114

Better to live a single day<br>seeing the supreme teaching<br>than to live a hundred years<br>blind to the supreme teaching. ^115

## 9. Wickedness ^vagga-9-0

Rush to do good,<br>shield your mind from evil;<br>for when you’re slow to do good,<br>your thoughts delight in wickedness. ^116

If you do something bad,<br>don’t do it again and again,<br>don’t set your heart on it,<br>for piling up evil is suffering. ^117

If you do something good,<br>do it again and again,<br>set your heart on it,<br>for piling up goodness is joyful. ^118

Even the wicked see good things,<br>so long as their wickedness has not ripened.<br>But as soon as that wickedness ripens,<br>then the wicked see wicked things. ^119

Even the good see wicked things,<br>so long as their goodness has not ripened.<br>But as soon as that goodness ripens,<br>then the good see good things. ^120

Think not lightly of evil,<br>that it won’t come back to you.<br>The pot is filled with water<br>falling drop by drop;<br>the fool is filled with wickedness<br>piled up bit by bit. ^121

Think not lightly of goodness,<br>that it won’t come back to you.<br>The pot is filled with water<br>falling drop by drop;<br>the attentive one is filled with goodness<br>piled up bit by bit. ^122

Avoid wickedness,<br>as a merchant with rich cargo and small escort<br>would avoid a dangerous road,<br>or one who loves life would avoid drinking poison. ^123

You can carry poison in your hand<br>if it has no wound,<br>for poison does not infect without a wound;<br>nothing bad happens unless you do bad. ^124

Whoever wrongs a man who has done no wrong,<br>a pure man who has not a blemish,<br>the evil backfires on the fool,<br>like fine dust thrown upwind. ^125

Some are born in a womb;<br>evildoers go to hell;<br>the virtuous go to heaven;<br>the undefiled are fully extinguished. ^126

Not in midair, nor mid-ocean,<br>nor hiding in a mountain cleft;<br>you’ll find no place on the planet<br>to escape your wicked deeds. ^127

Not in midair, nor mid-ocean,<br>nor hiding in a mountain cleft;<br>you’ll find no place on the planet<br>where you won’t be vanquished by death. ^128

## 10. The Rod ^vagga-10-0

All tremble at the rod,<br>all fear death.<br>Treating others like oneself,<br>neither kill nor incite to kill. ^129

All tremble at the rod,<br>all love life.<br>Treating others like oneself,<br>neither kill nor incite to kill. ^130

Creatures love happiness,<br>so if you harm them with a stick<br>in search of your own happiness,<br>after death you won’t find happiness. ^131

Creatures love happiness,<br>so if you don’t harm them with a stick<br>in search of your own happiness,<br>after death you will find happiness. ^132

Don’t speak harshly,<br>they may speak harshly back.<br>For aggressive speech is painful,<br>and the rod may spring back on you. ^133

If you still yourself<br>like a broken gong,<br>you reach extinguishment<br>and know no conflict. ^134

As a cowherd drives the cows<br>to pasture with the rod,<br>so too old age and death<br>drive life from living beings. ^135

The fool does not understand<br>the evil that they do.<br>But because of those deeds, that simpleton<br>is tormented as if burnt by fire. ^136

One who violently attacks<br>the peaceful and the innocent<br>swiftly falls<br>to one of ten bad states: ^137

harsh pain; loss;<br>the breakup of the body;<br>serious illness;<br>mental distress; ^138

hazards from rulers;<br>vicious slander;<br>loss of kin;<br>destruction of wealth; ^139

or else their home<br>is consumed by fire.<br>When their body breaks up, that witless person<br>is reborn in hell. ^140

Not nudity, nor matted hair, nor mud,<br>nor fasting, nor lying on bare ground,<br>nor wearing dust and dirt, or squatting on the heels,<br>will cleanse a mortal not free of doubt. ^141

Dressed up they may be, but if they live well—<br>peaceful, tamed, committed to the spiritual path,<br>having laid aside violence towards all creatures—<br>they are a brahmin, an ascetic, a mendicant. ^142

Can a person constrained by conscience<br>be found in the world?<br>Who shies away from blame,<br>like a fine horse from the whip? ^143

Like a fine horse under the whip,<br>be keen and full of urgency.<br>With faith, ethics, and energy,<br>immersion, and investigation of principles,<br>accomplished in knowledge and conduct, mindful,<br>give up this vast suffering. ^144

While irrigators guide water,<br>fletchers shape arrows,<br>and carpenters carve timber—<br>those true to their vows tame themselves. ^145

## 11. Old Age ^vagga-11-0

What is joy, what is laughter,<br>when the flames are ever burning?<br>Swathed in darkness,<br>would you not seek a light? ^146

See this fancy puppet,<br>a body built of sores,<br>diseased, obsessed over,<br>in which nothing lasts at all. ^147

This body is decrepit and frail,<br>a nest of disease.<br>This foul carcass falls apart,<br>for life ends in death. ^148

These dove-grey bones<br>are tossed away like<br>dried gourds in the autumn—<br>what joy is there in such a sight? ^149

In this city built of bones,<br>plastered with flesh and blood,<br>old age and death are stashed away,<br>along with conceit and contempt. ^150

Fancy chariots of kings wear out,<br>and even this body gets old.<br>But the truth of the good never gets old—<br>so the good proclaim to the good. ^151

A person of little learning<br>ages like an ox—<br>their flesh grows,<br>but not their wisdom. ^152

Transmigrating through countless rebirths,<br>I’ve journeyed without reward,<br>searching for the house-builder;<br>painful is birth again and again. ^153

I’ve seen you, house-builder!<br>You won’t build a house again!<br>Your rafters are all broken,<br>your roof-peak demolished.<br>The mind, set on demolition,<br>has reached the end of cravings. ^154

When young they spurned the spiritual path<br>and failed to earn any wealth.<br>Now they brood like old cranes<br>in a pond bereft of fish. ^155

When young they spurned the spiritual path<br>and failed to earn any wealth.<br>Now they lie like spent arrows,<br>bemoaning over things past. ^156

## 12. The Self ^vagga-12-0

If you knew your self as beloved,<br>you’d look after it so well.<br>In one of the night’s three watches,<br>an astute person would remain alert. ^157

The astute would avoid being corrupted<br>by first grounding themselves<br>in what is suitable,<br>and then instructing others. ^158

If one were to treat oneself<br>as one instructs another,<br>the well-tamed indeed would tame:<br>for the self, it seems, is hard to tame. ^159

One is indeed the lord of oneself,<br>for who else would be one’s lord?<br>By means of a well-tamed self,<br>one gains a lord that’s rare indeed. ^160

For the evil that is done by oneself,<br>born and produced in oneself,<br>grinds down a simpleton,<br>as diamond grinds a lesser gem. ^161

One choked by immorality,<br>as a sal tree by a creeper,<br>does to themselves<br>what a foe only wishes. ^162

It’s easy to do bad things<br>harmful to oneself,<br>but good things that are helpful<br>are the hardest things to do. ^163

On account of wicked views—<br>scorning the guidance<br>of the perfected ones,<br>the noble ones living righteously—<br>the idiot begets their own self’s demise,<br>like the bamboo bearing fruit. ^164

For it is by oneself that evil’s done,<br>one is corrupted by oneself.<br>It’s by oneself that evil’s not done,<br>one is purified by oneself.<br>Purity and impurity are personal matters,<br>no one can purify another. ^165

Never neglect what is good for yourself<br>for the sake of another, however great.<br>Knowing well what is good for yourself,<br>be intent upon your heart’s goal. ^166

## 13. The World ^vagga-13-0

Don’t resort to lowly things,<br>don’t abide in negligence,<br>don’t resort to wrong views,<br>don’t perpetuate the world. ^167

Get up, don’t be heedless,<br>live by principle, with good conduct.<br>For one of good conduct sleeps at ease,<br>in this world and the next. ^168

Live by principle, with good conduct,<br>don’t conduct yourself badly.<br>For one of good conduct sleeps at ease,<br>in this world and the next. ^169

Look upon the world<br>as a bubble<br>or a mirage,<br>then the King of Death won’t see you. ^170

Come, see this world decked out<br>like a fancy royal chariot.<br>Here fools founder,<br>but the discerning are not chained. ^171

He who once was heedless,<br>but turned to heedfulness,<br>lights up the world<br>like the moon freed from clouds. ^172

## 25. Mendicants ^vagga-25-0

Restraint of the eye is good;<br>good is restraint of the ear;<br>restraint of the nose is good;<br>good is restraint of the tongue. ^360

One restrained in hand and foot,<br>and in speech, the supreme restraint;<br>happy inside, serene,<br>solitary, content, I call a mendicant. ^362

One who has no sense of ownership<br>in the whole realm of name and form,<br>who does not grieve for that which is not,<br>is said to be a mendicant. ^367

A mendicant who meditates on love,<br>devoted to the Buddha’s teaching,<br>would realize the peaceful state,<br>the blissful stilling of conditions. ^368

Bail out this boat, mendicant!<br>When bailed out it will float lightly.<br>Having cut off desire and hate,<br>you shall reach extinguishment. ^369

Five to cut, five to drop,<br>and five more to develop.<br>When a mendicant slips five chains<br>they’re said to have crossed the flood. ^370

Practice absorption, don’t be negligent!<br>Don’t let the mind delight in the senses!<br>Don’t heedlessly swallow a hot iron ball!<br>And when it burns, don’t cry, “Oh, the pain!” ^371

No absorption for one without wisdom,<br>no wisdom for one without absorption.<br>But one with absorption and wisdom—<br>they have truly drawn near to extinguishment. ^372

A mendicant who enters an empty hut<br>with mind at peace<br>finds a superhuman delight<br>as they rightly discern the Dhamma. ^373

Whenever they are mindful<br>of the rise and fall of the aggregates,<br>they feel rapture and joy:<br>that is freedom from death for one who knows. ^374

As a jasmine sheds<br>its withered flowers,<br>O mendicants,<br>shed greed and hate. ^377

## 26. Brahmins ^vagga-26-0
